The Sampson-Kahn Family

Kept by RoRo - Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, 1907-1997

A Note from RoRo

I'm Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. Born in Oakland in 1907, raised in a family that helped build this city. My father Frederick ran Kahn Brothers Department Store at 16th and Broadway, that grand building with the dome. My mother Helen came from the Lavensons. I married John Sampson in 1931. We had three children, seven grandchildren, and I lived to see my great-grandchildren born.

This is our family tree. Jean Wildberg and I researched it for years. Some lines go back to Germany in the 1820s, others to Charleston and Georgetown before the Civil War. There's a possible Sephardic connection through the Sampsons that traces to Portugal in the 1400s, but the evidence has gaps. I've marked what's verified and what's still theory.

The family has been in California since the Gold Rush. We were merchants, doctors, educators, philanthropists. We built stores and hospitals, founded temples, served on boards. We were part of Oakland and San Francisco when both cities were young.

The Kahns: From Darmstadt to Oakland

My grandfather Israel Kahn came from Darmstadt, Hesse, in 1848. He was twenty-six. He sailed from Rotterdam on the Henry Shelton and landed in Baltimore. His parents were Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Goldstein.

Israel married Sarah Bauer. They had five sons: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, John, and my father Frederick. In 1879, Israel founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. When he died in 1883, his sons took over.

My father Frederick was born in New York in 1860. He worked first at Bauer Brothers Company in San Francisco, then joined the family store. The business became Kahn Brothers Department Store. In 1912 they moved to a Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway, designed by Charles W. Dickey. It had a 120-foot elliptical dome. You can still see it. It's the Rotunda Building now, an Oakland landmark.

Frederick was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai. He chaired the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education during a five million dollar school building program. He was vice president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. He belonged to the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith.

He died in Oakland in 1925 at our home on Harrison Street. He's buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery.

The Lavensons: My Mother's Family

My mother was Helen Lavenson. Her parents were Samuel Lavenson and Caroline Heiman. Caroline died on June 24, 1872, in Sacramento. She was only twenty-nine or thirty. My mother was born that same year. Helen had two older sisters, Clara and Sara, who were two and one when their mother died.

Helen's brother Albert S. Lavenson was born in Sacramento in 1865. He was partner and vice president of Capwell's Department Store for thirty years. He was president of Temple Sinai. He organized the Commercial Club, which became the Oakland Chamber of Commerce. He was a music patron at Mills College and helped build their Music Building. He died in 1930.

Albert married Amy Furth in 1894. Her father was Simon Furth, a Gold Rush merchant. Their daughter was Alma Ruth Lavenson, born in Oakland in 1897. Alma became a noted photographer. She studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham. She died in 1989.

After my father died in 1925, my mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 to the Oakland Museum. They used it to purchase what became the Kahn Collection, seventy-one paintings of nineteenth-century California art. Marjorie Arkelian catalogued them.

My mother died in Oakland in 1935.

Growing Up in Oakland

I was born December 10, 1907, in Oakland. I had a sister, Helen Sarah, born in 1913, and a brother, Frederick Jr. As a girl, I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication ceremony.

I went to UC Berkeley and graduated Phi Beta Kappa in 1928. After college I kept busy with the Kahn Foundation. I was an award-winning weaver. I wove the furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home and made a needlepoint cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House.

I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my eighties. I made weekly visits to nursery schools as "Grandma RoRo." I attended services and concerts at Congregation Emanu-El.

My sister Helen married Josua Eppinger. She died in 1956. She was only forty-three.

The Sampsons: Georgetown and Charleston

My husband John's family came from South Carolina. His grandfather Joseph H. Sampson was born in Charleston in 1823. Joseph and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants in Georgetown. They had a mercantile business and did shipping. Georgetown had a Jewish community that went back to the 1700s. By 1800, Jews made up about ten percent of the white population. It was the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston.

The Sampson family names appear in the records of Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim in Charleston, one of the oldest synagogues in America.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen in 1850. Hetty was born in Charleston in 1832. Her father was Hartwig Cohen, who came from Silesia and was the spiritual leader at Beth Elohim. Her mother was Deborah Marks Cohen, born in Charleston in 1801. The Marks family name was anglicized from Marques.

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their business and expanded the shipping operation. Joseph died in 1883. He's buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, which was established in 1772.

The Sampson name comes from England, from Bury St. Edmunds. It's an anglicization of the Hebrew Shimshon, which means "sun." The Greeks added the p.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson

Joseph and Hetty's son Arthur Fischel Sampson was born in Georgetown in 1855. He went to medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. On April 4, 1887, he married Babette "Bettie" Levy, who was born in San Antonio in 1868. Her parents were Abraham Levy and Esther Halff.

Arthur and Bettie had five children in Galveston: Joseph Abraham in 1888, Arthur Jr. in 1891, Cornelia "Nellie" in 1893, Esther, and John Jacob in 1898.

In 1900, the Galveston hurricane destroyed much of the city. Arthur moved his family to San Francisco in 1902. They lived on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter. During the 1906 earthquake, the U.S. Army dynamited their home to create a firebreak against the fire. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur died in San Francisco in 1934. Bettie died in Santa Clara in 1949.

Grandpa John

John Jacob Sampson was born in Galveston on April 28, 1898. He got his medical degree before he was twenty-one. He spent three years at UC, then went to medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India.

He practiced with his father Arthur for a time, then joined the research and clinical staff at UCSF. He worked until he was eighty-five. He was Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital.

John was a cardiologist. He published forty-nine papers between 1926 and 1980. He did research on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He worked at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research. His key collaborators were Meyer Friedman, who defined Type A behavior, Herman Uhley, and Sanford Leeds.

After he died, UCSF established the John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology in his honor.

We were married May 17, 1931. John died December 24, 1987, in San Francisco. He was eighty-nine.

Our Life Together

John and I lived in San Francisco, in Seacliff. We had three children: Deborah in 1934, John Frederick in 1935, and Janet in 1937.

John kept working at Mount Zion and UCSF into his eighties. I volunteered at Mount Zion and kept up with the Kahn Foundation. We were members of Congregation Emanu-El.

I died July 12, 1997, at Mount Zion Hospital. A lung embolism. I was eighty-nine. My memorial service was August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

Our Children

Deborah

Deborah Sampson Green was born January 11, 1934, in San Francisco. She went to Lowell, Alamo, Presidio Junior High, and Wellesley. She became an educator and English literature professor. She married John R. Green. They live in Seattle. Their son Caleb married Kate Banta-Green. Caleb and Kate have two sons, Robert Bruce and Peter Caleb.

John Frederick

John Frederick Sampson was born March 18, 1935, in San Francisco. He went to Lowell High School, then Reed College for three years. He got his AB from the University of San Francisco in 1957 and went to Wharton. He's an appraiser and real estate broker in San Francisco.

He married Louise Adler in 1962. They divorced in 1991. They had three children: Jennifer Ruth, John Frederick Jr., and Juliet Margaret. In 1999, John married Sharon Litsky.

Janet

Janet Sampson Reider was born March 1, 1937, in San Francisco. She's a photographer. Her website is janetreider.com. She met Pete Reider at Lowell High School when she was thirteen. They were married in the spring of 1958.

Pete Reider

Arthur Elliott "Pete" Reider was born November 1, 1936, in Topeka, Kansas. His father Norman was training at the Menninger Clinic. Pete went to Presidio Hill School, Grant School, and Lowell High School. He went to Harvard with the Class of 1958, then Harvard Medical School.

At Harvard, Pete was captain of the track team. He ran a 4:11 mile in 1957, which was the Harvard record. He set two-mile records. He made the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team. His coach Bill McCurdy called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." Harvard put him in their Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982.

Pete became a psychiatrist. He had a private practice in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts. He retired in San Francisco. In 2014 he published Stepping Stones, a collection of poetry and fiction. He was a member of the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation Lifelong Learning Institute at Brandeis.

Pete died of lymphoma on August 13, 2015, at home in San Francisco with Janet and their three children. His memorial was August 17 at Congregation Emanu-El.

Pete's brother Jonathan was a Stanford admissions officer for fifteen years, then Director of College Counseling at San Francisco University High School.

Dr. Norman Reider

Pete's father Norman Reider was born July 3, 1907, in Alliance, Ohio. He got his BA and MD from Western Reserve University, which is now Case Western Reserve. He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939.

Norman moved to San Francisco in 1946 and became Chief of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital, succeeding Jascha Kasanin. He was an early member of the San Francisco Psychoanalytic Society, which was founded in 1942.

In 1947, TIME magazine featured Norman for his paper "The Unmarried Father." In 1959 he wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa." He was on the board of the ACLU in Northern California.

Norman married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. Louise was born in Cleveland on November 29, 1907. She was a psychiatric social worker. She stopped practicing around 1946.

Norman's papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis. Four and a half linear feet covering 1822 to the 1980s.

Norman died in San Francisco on March 23, 1989. Louise died February 22, 1985.

Norman's Parents

Norman's father was Maurice Jacob Reider, originally named Rudner. He was born around 1882 in Horoduk Village in the Vilna Gubernia, which was Lithuania under Russian rule. He came to America and worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio. He changed the family name from Rudner to Reider.

Maurice's father was Uriah "Uri" Rudner, born in 1855 in Orduk Village in Vilna. Uri married Clara Scope, who was born in Russia in 1862. They had several children: Anna, Morris (who became Maurice Reider), Isaac, Bennie, Len, Sam, and Rhea. Bennie died in 1901 at seventeen. Clara died in 1909. Uri later married Hattie Altman. Uri died in Canton in 1916.

Norman's mother was Lillian Cohen. She was born in 1887 and died in 1981. Her parents were Hyman Cohen, born in 1850, and Jennie Sugarman, born in 1865 and died in 1928.

Louise's father was Abe (Abba Elyia) Pasek Cohen, born in 1886. He died around 1915. His father was Aaron Cohen Pasek. Louise's mother was Rena Shepsenwol. She was born in 1886. Her parents were Max Cohen and a woman from the Levine Shepsenwol family.

The Sephardic Question

There's a possible line back to the Sephardic Jews of Portugal, but it's not proven.

The chain would go through Hetty Cohen's mother, Deborah Marks. Her maiden name was Marks, anglicized from Marques, which is a Portuguese name. Deborah's father was Samuel Mendel Marks. His father was Isaac Marks, born in 1732 in New York.

Isaac fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He married Miriam Simpson (or Simons) from Long Island. His son Samuel Mendel moved to Charleston.

Multiple genealogy trees list Isaac's mother as Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, born December 16, 1699, in Bordeaux. She died July 30, 1759, in Curaçao and is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery there, section IV, row C, grave 621. But Isaac's father is unknown.

Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722, in Barbados. Jacob died around 1725. Isaac wasn't born until 1732. So if Esther was his mother, we don't know who his father was. She later married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro, who is buried next to her in Curaçao.

Bernard Baruch, the Wall Street financier, was a cousin through Isaac Marks's granddaughter Sarah Wolfe. In his autobiography, Baruch claimed descent from Isaac Rodriguez Marques. But the dates don't work for a direct line.

Matt researched this extensively in 2022. He corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at the Beth Haim cemetery in Curaçao and looked at Isaac S. Emmanuel's genealogical records. Emmanuel's own books contain contradictions about Esther's marriages.

If the Sephardic connection holds, Esther's line goes back through rabbis in Amsterdam to Portugal in the 1400s.

Her father was Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo, a rabbi born in Amsterdam in 1673. His father was Hazzan and Ribi Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro, also a rabbi and cantor, born in Amsterdam in 1649. Mosseh married Hester Dias Sardo on July 13, 1670. He arrived in Curaçao in 1672 and died there in 1708.

Mosseh's father was Salomon (Salomão) de Moshe Levy Maduro, born in Amsterdam in 1629. He married Chana de Castro on October 27, 1648.

Salomon's father was Mosseh Levy o Maduro, born around 1591 in Portugal. He moved to Amsterdam and adopted the Maduro name. He married Clara Rodriguez on July 27, 1622. Mosseh died in Amsterdam on January 1, 1640.

The line goes back through several more generations of Levys to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in Portugal in the 1400s.

But the connection between Isaac Marks and Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is not proven. It's plausible, but the documentation is missing. So we note it as theory.

The Family's Places

Oakland

Israel Kahn founded his dry goods business here in 1879. Frederick expanded it into Kahn Brothers Department Store at 16th and Broadway, in that beautiful domed building that's still a landmark. The Lavensons were here too. Albert ran Capwell's and helped organize the Chamber of Commerce. Frederick and Albert both served as president of Temple Sinai. This was our family's city. I was born here in 1907.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Joseph H. Sampson and his brother Samuel ran their mercantile and shipping business here after the Civil War. The Jewish community dated back to the 1700s. Joseph is buried in the Jewish Cemetery, established in 1772. Hetty Cohen Sampson died here in 1897.

Charleston, South Carolina

The Sampsons and Cohens came from Charleston. Joseph H. Sampson was born here in 1823. Hetty Cohen was born here in 1832. Her father Hartwig was the spiritual leader at Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim. Her mother Deborah Marks Cohen was born here in 1801. Charleston had one of the oldest Jewish communities in America.

Galveston, Texas

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine here. He married Bettie Levy here on April 4, 1887. Their five children were born here: Joseph Abraham in 1888, Arthur Jr. in 1891, Nellie in 1893, Esther, and John in 1898. The 1900 hurricane destroyed much of the city. Arthur moved his family to San Francisco in 1902.

San Francisco

Arthur and Bettie Sampson moved here after the Galveston flood. Their Franklin Street home was dynamited during the 1906 earthquake as a firebreak. They rebuilt on Washington Street. John went to medical school here and spent his career at UCSF and Mount Zion Hospital. We lived in Seacliff. We were members of Congregation Emanu-El. Both John and I died at Mount Zion Hospital. Our memorial services were at Emanu-El.

Curaçao

If the Sephardic line is real, this is where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro died in 1759. She's buried at Beth Haim Cemetery. Her husband Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried next to her. The cemetery has records going back to the 1650s. Matt corresponded with the staff there in 2022.

What We've Built

This family came from Germany, from South Carolina, from Lithuania, from Portugal if the Sephardic line holds. We were merchants and doctors and educators. We built department stores with domed ceilings. We served as chiefs of staff at hospitals and presidents of temples. We founded welfare federations and chaired school boards and sat on museum boards.

We collected California art and endowed chairs in cardiology. We ran track at Harvard and photographed the West and directed college counseling and built health IT companies. We've had deans of law schools and global executives and family physicians.

The family has been in California for over 170 years. We helped build Oakland and San Francisco when they were young cities.

I kept the records. This is what I know.

How This Was Built

This site is generated by a WhatsApp bot named RoRo, after my grandmother Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. The idea started after I read a blog post about building a family tree AI assistant that connected a GEDCOM file to a WhatsApp bot using a graph database. I thought: I could do something like that for our family.

I already had a WhatsApp bot running on OpenClaw, an open-source framework for building AI personas on messaging platforms. My brother and I had been using it to chat with "Pepper," a bot modeled after our childhood dog. Pepper was just there for fun, nothing serious. But after reading that blog post, I wanted to build something more useful, something the whole family could interact with around a topic that actually matters to us: our history.

The first version of RoRo had personality but not much substance. I had some family facts saved in a couple of markdown files, and she ran as an OpenClaw agent. I asked her a dozen times, "Who are your grandparents?" and she had no idea. The information was too loosely structured for her to work with.

So I rebuilt her from the ground up. Instead of OpenClaw's higher-level framework, I went directly to the libraries it wraps: Baileys for WhatsApp connectivity and the Anthropic SDK for Claude as the language model. I moved the family data into a structured JSON file with proper fields for names, dates, places, relationships, and sources. That made a huge difference. RoRo could suddenly answer questions accurately and tell coherent stories about the family.

Next, I connected her to Geni.com through a small app I built for their API. This lets her look things up, validate what she knows, and push updates back when family members share new information. She can also export the tree as a GEDCOM file for use in other genealogy software.

Today, RoRo listens to our WhatsApp family group, answers questions about our history, and can update the family tree interactively. Changes get saved to the JSON, synced to Geni, exported to GEDCOM, and published to this site. She generates the narrative on the "Our Story" tab using Claude, and the whole site is deployed automatically through GitHub Pages.

I am about to introduce her new self to the family, with all these improvements. Wish us luck.
