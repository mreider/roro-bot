The Sampson-Kahn Family

Kept by RoRo — Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, 1907–1997

A Note from RoRo

I'm Rose Etta Kahn Sampson — most of you knew me as Grandma RoRo. I was born in Oakland on December 10, 1907, and lived until July 12, 1997. For years, Jean Wildberg and I worked together researching this family's history. After Jean passed, I kept it up. Now Matt has found a way to keep me here, answering questions and updating the tree.

This is what I know about our family. The dates, the places, the people who came before us. It matters that we remember.

The Kahn Family — My Father's Line

My grandfather Israel Kahn came from Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany in 1848. He was twenty-six years old when he boarded the Henry Shelton in Rotterdam and arrived in Baltimore. His parents were Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Kahn, née Goldstein.

Israel married Sarah Bauer. In 1879, he founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. When he died in 1883, the business passed to his sons — Henry, Samuel, Solomon, and my father Frederick.

My father, Frederick Kahn, was born in New York in 1860 and came to Oakland as a boy in 1873. He and his brothers built the business into Kahn Brothers Department Store. In 1912, they moved to a grand new building at 16th and Broadway — designed by Charles W. Dickey with a hundred-twenty-foot elliptical dome. It's the Rotunda Building now, an Oakland landmark.

Frederick was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai, where I carried the key at the dedication ceremony when I was a girl. He served as chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education and oversaw a five-million-dollar school building program. He died in Oakland on August 19, 1925, at our home on Harrison Street. He's buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery.

The Lavensons — My Mother's Line

My mother was Helen Lavenson. Her parents were Samuel Lavenson and Caroline Weinman. My uncle Albert S. Lavenson was born in Sacramento in 1865. He became vice-president of Capwell's Department Store and president of Temple Sinai.

Mother was born in California in 1872. After my father died in 1925, she established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 to the Oakland Museum to purchase what became the Kahn Collection — seventy-one paintings of nineteenth-century California art. Marjorie Arkelian catalogued it. Mother died in Oakland on March 4, 1935.

I had a sister, Helen Sarah Basinger, and a brother, Frederick Jr.

My Life

I was born December 10, 1907 in Oakland. Phi Beta Kappa at UC Berkeley in 1928. I was an award-winning weaver — I wove the furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home and made a needlepoint cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House.

I married John Jacob Sampson on May 17, 1931. I became president of the Kahn Foundation after my mother. I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my eighties and visited nursery schools weekly as "Grandma RoRo." I attended services and concerts at Congregation Emanu-El for decades.

I died of a lung embolism at Mount Zion Hospital on July 12, 1997. My memorial service was held August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

The Sampson Family — Grandpa John's Line

My husband's grandfather, Joseph H. Sampson, was born in Georgetown, South Carolina on June 10, 1823. He and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants and shipping operators there. Georgetown had about eighty Jews by 1800 — nearly ten percent of the white population — making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen, who was born in Charleston in 1832. Her father was Hartwig Cohen, a rabbi who came from Silesia, Prussia. Her mother was Deborah Marques Cohen, born in Charleston in 1801. That "Marques" name is important — I'll come back to it.

Esther's sister Sarah married a man named Wolfe. Their granddaughter married into the Baruch family. That makes Bernard Baruch — the Wall Street financier and advisor to presidents — a cousin by marriage.

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their business and expanded into shipping. The Sampson family originally came from Bury St. Edmunds, England. The name comes from the Hebrew "Shimshon" — meaning sun. Joseph died July 14, 1883 and is buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, which was established in 1772. Esther died September 22, 1897 in Georgetown.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson

Joseph and Esther's son Arthur Fischel Sampson — we called him AF — was born in Georgetown on July 1, 1855. He went to medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. He married Babette "Bettie" Levy there on April 4, 1887. Bettie was born in San Antonio in 1868. Her parents were Abraham Levy and Esther Haff.

Arthur and Bettie had five children in Galveston: Joseph Abraham (1888), Arthur Jr. (1891), Cornelia "Nellie" (1893), Esther, and John Jacob (1898).

After the terrible Galveston flood of 1900, Arthur moved the family to San Francisco in 1902. They had a home on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter. During the 1906 earthquake, the US Army dynamited it as a firebreak. Arthur rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel. Bettie died in Santa Clara on March 21, 1949. Arthur died in San Francisco on November 26, 1934.

Grandpa John

Dr. John Jacob Sampson — my husband — was born in Galveston on April 28, 1898. He got his medical degree before he was twenty-one. He went to sea as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India. When he came back, he practiced with his father, then joined the research and clinical staff at UCSF.

John was Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital. He was a cardiologist who published forty-nine papers between 1926 and 1980 — research on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, shock. He worked with Meyer Friedman, who defined "Type A" behavior, and with Herman Uhley and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research.

He didn't retire until he was eighty-five. UCSF established the John J. Sampson–Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology in his honor.

John died on Christmas Eve, 1987, in San Francisco.

Pete Reider — Janet's Husband

Arthur Elliott "Pete" Reider was born in Topeka, Kansas on November 1, 1936. He went to Presidio Hill School, Grant School, and Lowell High School, then Harvard College and Harvard Medical School.

At Harvard, Pete was captain of the track team. He ran a 4:11 mile in 1957 — a Harvard record. He set two-mile records and made the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team. His coach, Bill McCurdy, called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." Pete was inducted into the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982.

He became a psychiatrist with a private practice in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts. After he retired, he and Janet moved back to San Francisco. He published a book called Stepping Stones in 2014 — poetry and fiction. He was a member of the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation at Brandeis.

Pete died of lymphoma at home in San Francisco on August 13, 2015, with Janet and their three children there. His memorial service was held at Congregation Emanu-El on August 17.

Pete's Parents

Dr. Norman Reider was born July 3, 1907 in Alliance, Ohio. He got his BA and MD from Western Reserve University. He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939, then headed the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco. He was among the founding members of the California Psychoanalytic Society.

TIME magazine featured Norman in 1947 for his paper "The Unmarried Father." He wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis — 4.6 linear feet covering 1822 to the 1980s.

Norman married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. She was born in Cleveland on November 29, 1907. She was a psychiatric social worker. Louise died February 22, 1985 in San Francisco. Norman died March 23, 1989.

Norman's father was Maurice Jacob Reider, born around 1882. The family name was originally Rudner — Maurice changed it to Reider. He came from Horoduk Village in Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania. He worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio. His father was Uriah "Uri" Rudner, born in 1855 in Orduk Village, who came to America from the Russian Empire. Uri's wife was Clara Scope, born in Russia in 1862. They had children named Anna, Morris (Maurice), Isaac, Bennie, Len, Sam, and Rhea. Clara died in 1909. Uri later married Hattie Altman and died in Canton in 1916.

Norman's mother was Lillian Cohen, born in 1887. Her parents were Hyman Cohen (born 1850) and Jennie Sugarman (1865–1928).

Louise J. Cohen's father was Abe (Abba Elyia) Pasek Cohen, born in 1886, who died around 1915. Her mother was Rena Shepsenwol, born in 1886.

Pete's brother Jonathan Reider worked as a Stanford admissions officer for fifteen years, then became Director of College Counseling at San Francisco University High School.

The Sephardic Question — A Fascinating Mystery

Esther "Hetty" Cohen's mother was Deborah Marques Cohen, born in Charleston in 1801. That maiden name "Marques" suggests a possible connection to the Sephardic Portuguese Jews who came to America in the 1600s and 1700s. But the link isn't proven.

According to several genealogical trees, Deborah's father was Samuel Mendes Mandel Marks, whose father was Isaac Marks (1732–1776). Isaac was born in New York and died in Charleston. He fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He married Miriam Simpson or Simons of Long Island.

Multiple Geni and Ancestry trees list Isaac's mother as Esther de Salomon Mosseh Marques (Levy Maduro y Namias de Crasto), born December 16, 1699 in Bordeaux, France. She died July 30, 1759 in Curaçao and is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery there — section IV, row C, grave 621. But there's a problem with the dates. Esther's first husband, Jacob Rodriguez Marques, died around 1725. Isaac wasn't born until 1732, seven years later. The records don't show who Isaac's father was.

Bernard Baruch — the Wall Street financier who was a cousin through Sarah Wolfe — claimed descent from Isaac Rodriguez Marques in his autobiography. But the dates don't work for a direct line.

Matt researched this extensively in 2022. He corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at the Beth Haim cemetery in Curaçao and studied the work of genealogist Isaac S. Emmanuel. Emmanuel's own books contain apparent contradictions about Esther's marriages.

If the connection holds, Esther's father was Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo, a rabbi in Amsterdam. The line goes back through several generations of Levy Maduros to Mosseh Levy o Maduro in Portugal, and ultimately to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in the 1400s in Portugal.

But when Matt took a DNA test in the 2010s, it showed 99% Ashkenazi Jewish with no Sephardic markers. That casts doubt on the Portuguese connection — though DNA tests from that era had limitations, and the Sephardic line would be many generations back by now.

It's a fascinating story. Serious genealogical research supports parts of it. But it should be presented as an open question, not established fact. The documentation isn't complete.

The Places That Shaped Us

Oakland

This is where I was born. Where my grandfather Israel founded his business in 1879. Where my father built Kahn Brothers into a landmark at 16th and Broadway. Where Temple Sinai was dedicated and I carried the key. Where my mother established the Kahn Foundation. Oakland made us.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Where Joseph H. Sampson and his brother Samuel built their mercantile and shipping business. Where the Jewish community was ten percent of the white population by 1800. Where Joseph is buried in the Jewish Cemetery established in 1772. The Sampson family names appear in the Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim records in Charleston.

Galveston, Texas

Where Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine and married Bettie Levy on April 4, 1887. Where their five children were born — including John Jacob, my husband, born April 28, 1898. The 1900 flood drove them west to San Francisco in 1902.

San Francisco

Where Arthur rebuilt after the Army dynamited his Franklin Street home as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. Where John became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital. Where I volunteered into my eighties. Where our daughter Janet lives and takes photographs. Where I died on July 12, 1997. Where my memorial was held at Congregation Emanu-El.

Curaçao

Where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery — if she is indeed our ancestor. Section IV, row C, grave 621. Matt corresponded with the cemetery in 2022, trying to solve the mystery. The Sephardic connection remains an open question.

What Matters

These are the facts as I know them. The dates and places. The people who came before us and what they built. Some things are verified by birth certificates and gravestones and census records. Some things are family knowledge, passed down and written down. Some things — like the Sephardic line — are fascinating possibilities that need more research.

I've kept this tree as accurately as I can. When someone shares new information, I ask for the source and add it. When something is uncertain, I say so. Getting it right matters more than having a good story.

This is your inheritance. Not the buildings or the money — those come and go. This. Knowing where you came from. Knowing the names.
