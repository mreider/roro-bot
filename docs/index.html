The Sampson-Kahn Family

Kept by RoRo - Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, 1907-1997

A Note from RoRo

I'm Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. Born in Oakland in 1907, died in San Francisco in 1997. I kept the family history for most of my life, researching it with Jean Wildberg, tracking down records, verifying dates. This is what I know about our family.

The Kahns came from Germany. The Sampsons from South Carolina and England before that. We built businesses, practiced medicine, kept our faith. Some of the deep ancestry is still being worked out, particularly the Sephardic connection through the Marques line. Matt researched that extensively. I'll tell you what's confirmed and what remains theory.

The Kahn Family: From Darmstadt to Oakland

My grandfather Israel Kahn arrived in Baltimore in 1848 from Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany, aboard the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. He was twenty-six years old. His parents were Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Kahn, born Goldstein.

Israel married Sarah Bauer. They had five sons: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, John, and my father Frederick. In 1879, Israel founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. When he died in 1883, his sons took over.

My father Frederick Kahn was born in New York in 1860. He joined his father's business at thirteen. The store became Kahn Brothers Department Store. In 1912 they moved to a grand Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway, designed by Charles W. Dickey with a 120-foot elliptical dome. It's now the Rotunda Building, an Oakland landmark on the National Register of Historic Places.

Frederick was more than a merchant. He was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. A founding member and president of Temple Sinai. I carried the key at the dedication ceremony when I was a girl. He chaired the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education and oversaw a five-million-dollar school building program. He served as vice president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. He belonged to the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith.

He died August 19, 1925, at our home on 3933 Harrison Street in Oakland. He's buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery.

The Lavensons: My Mother's Family

My mother was Helen Lavenson, born in California in 1872. Her parents were Samuel Lavenson and Caroline Weinman. Her brother Albert S. Lavenson was born in Sacramento in 1865. He became vice president of Capwell's Department Store and president of Temple Sinai.

After my father died in 1925, my mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 to the Oakland Museum to purchase what became the Kahn Collection: seventy-one paintings of nineteenth-century California art, catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian.

My mother died March 4, 1935, in Oakland.

The Sampson Family: Georgetown and Charleston

Joseph H. Sampson, my husband's grandfather, was born in Georgetown, South Carolina, in 1823. He and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants and shipping operators there. By 1800, Georgetown had about eighty Jews, roughly ten percent of the white population. It was the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston.

The Sampson family names appear in the records of Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim in Charleston. They originally came from Bury St. Edmunds, England. The surname is an anglicization of the Hebrew Shimshon, meaning sun. The p was introduced in the Greek transliteration.

Joseph married Esther Cohen, called Hetty, born in Charleston in 1832. Her parents were Hartwig Cohen, a rabbi born in Wartha, Silesia, Prussia in 1783, and Deborah Marques Cohen, born in Charleston in 1801. Deborah's maiden name Marques is the key to the Sephardic question I'll discuss later.

Esther's siblings included Sarah Cohen, who married a man named Wolfe. Sarah was the grandmother of Bernard Baruch, the Wall Street financier and presidential advisor. We're cousins through that line.

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping. Joseph died July 14, 1883, and is buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772. Esther died in Georgetown in 1897.

From South Carolina to Texas to San Francisco

Joseph and Esther's son Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson, called AF, was born in Georgetown in 1855. He went to medical school in South Carolina and moved to Galveston, Texas, to practice. On April 4, 1887, he married Babette Levy, called Bettie, born in San Antonio in 1868. Her parents were Abraham Levy and Esther Haff.

Arthur and Bettie had five children in Galveston: Joseph Abraham in 1888, Arthur Jr. in 1891, Cornelia (Nellie) in 1893, Esther, and John Jacob in 1898.

The 1900 Galveston flood devastated the city. In 1902, Arthur moved the family to San Francisco. They lived on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter. During the 1906 earthquake, the U.S. Army dynamited their home as a firebreak to stop the fire from spreading. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur died in San Francisco in 1934. Bettie died in Santa Clara in 1949.

My Life with Grandpa John

I was born Rose Etta Kahn on December 10, 1907, in Oakland. I graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. I was an award-winning weaver. I wove the furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home and made a needlepoint cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House.

I married Dr. John Jacob Sampson on May 17, 1931. Everyone called him Grandpa John. He was born in Galveston on April 28, 1898, the youngest of Arthur and Bettie's children.

John earned his medical degree before he was twenty-one. He spent three years at UC, then went straight to medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India. He practiced with his father, then joined the research and clinical staff at UCSF. He worked until he was eighty-five.

John was Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital. As a cardiologist, he published forty-nine papers between 1926 and 1980 on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman, the man who defined Type A behavior, and with Herman Uhley and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research.

UCSF established the John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology in his honor.

I served as president of the Kahn Foundation. I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my eighties, made weekly visits to nursery schools as Grandma RoRo, and attended services and concerts at Congregation Emanu-El.

John died December 24, 1987, in San Francisco. I died July 12, 1997, of a lung embolism at Mount Zion Hospital. My memorial service was August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

Pete Reider: Janet's Husband

Arthur Elliott Reider, called Pete, was born November 1, 1936, in Topeka, Kansas. He attended Presidio Hill School, Grant School, and Lowell High School in San Francisco, then Harvard, Class of 1958. He went to Harvard Medical School.

Pete was captain of the Harvard track team. He ran a 4:11 mile, which was the Harvard record in 1957. He set two-mile records. His coach, Bill McCurdy, called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." Pete was inducted into the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982 and named to the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team.

Pete became a psychiatrist. He had a private practice in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts, before retiring in San Francisco. He published Stepping Stones, a collection of poetry and fiction, in 2014. He was a member of the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation Lifelong Learning Institute at Brandeis.

Pete died of lymphoma on August 13, 2015, at home in San Francisco with Janet and their three children. His memorial service was August 17, 2015, at Congregation Emanu-El.

Pete's father was Dr. Norman Reider, born in Alliance, Ohio, in 1907. Norman earned his BA and MD from Western Reserve University, now Case Western Reserve. He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939. He headed the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital and was among the founding members of the California Psychoanalytic Society. TIME magazine featured him in 1947 for his paper "The Unmarried Father." He also wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis. Norman died in San Francisco in 1989.

Norman married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. Louise was a psychiatric social worker who stopped practicing around 1946. She died in San Francisco in 1985.

Pete's brother Jonathan Reider was a Stanford admissions officer for fifteen years, then Director of College Counseling at San Francisco University High School.

The Sephardic Question: An Open Mystery

There's a possible Sephardic Portuguese line through Deborah Marques Cohen, Esther Cohen's mother. The maiden name Marques suggests a connection to the Sephardic Marks or Marques family, but the link isn't proven.

The chain goes like this. Deborah's father was Samuel Mendes Mandel Marks. His father was Isaac Marks, born in New York in 1732, died in Charleston in 1776. Isaac fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He married Miriam Simpson or Simons of Long Island.

Multiple genealogy trees list Isaac's mother as Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, also called Esther Marques, born December 16, 1699, in Bordeaux, France, died July 30, 1759, in Curaçao. She's buried at Beth Haim Cemetery in Curaçao, section IV, row C, grave 621. But Isaac's father is unknown.

Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722, in Barbados. Jacob died around 1725. Isaac was born in 1732, seven years later. The original documentation for this maternal link is unclear. Matt researched this extensively in 2022 and corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at the Beth Haim cemetery and looked into the work of genealogist Isaac S. Emmanuel, whose books contain apparent contradictions about Esther's marriages.

Bernard Baruch, our cousin through Sarah Cohen Wolfe, claimed descent from Isaac Rodriguez Marques in his autobiography, but the dates don't work for a direct line.

If the connection holds, the Levy Maduro line goes back through rabbis in Amsterdam to Portugal in the 1400s. Esther's father was Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo of Amsterdam. The line continues back through several generations of Levy Maduros to Mosseh Levy o Maduro in Portugal, and ultimately to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in Portugal in the 1400s.

However, Matt's DNA test from the 2010s showed 99% Ashkenazi Jewish with no Sephardic markers, which casts doubt on the Portuguese connection. DNA tests from that era had limitations, and the Sephardic line would be many generations back. The story is fascinating family lore and serious genealogical research, but it should be presented as an open question, not established fact.

The Places That Shaped Us

Oakland

Where I was born and raised. Where my grandfather Israel built his business at 10th and Broadway. Where my father helped build Temple Sinai and oversaw the schools. Where the Rotunda Building still stands at 16th and Broadway. Where my parents are buried at Mountain View Cemetery.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Where the Sampsons were merchants and shippers. Where Joseph H. Sampson is buried in the Jewish cemetery established in 1772. A small port town that was ten percent Jewish by 1800, the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston.

Galveston, Texas

Where Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine. Where he and Bettie raised their five children. Where Grandpa John was born in 1898. The 1900 flood changed everything and sent the family west to San Francisco.

San Francisco

Where John and I made our life together. Where we raised our three children in Seacliff. Where John was Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital and I volunteered there for decades. Where we attended Congregation Emanu-El. Where we're both buried.

Curaçao

Where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery, if the Sephardic connection is real. A thread that might tie us back to Bordeaux, Amsterdam, and Portugal, or might be a genealogical puzzle we'll never fully solve.
