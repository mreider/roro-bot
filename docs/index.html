The Sampson-Kahn Family

The Sampson-Kahn family descends from German-Jewish immigrants who settled in California in the mid-19th century and Southern Jewish merchants with roots in Charleston and Georgetown, South Carolina. The families joined through the 1931 marriage of Rose Etta Kahn and Dr. John Jacob Sampson in San Francisco.

Kahn Origins: Germany to Oakland

Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Goldstein, born in Germany in the early 1800s, had at least one son: Israel Kahn, born in Darmstadt, Hesse in 1822. Israel emigrated from Germany in 1848, arriving in Baltimore aboard the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. He married Sarah Bauer (1828-1888) and they had eight children: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, Frederick, John, Hannah, Bertha, Rosetta, and Henrietta.

Israel founded a dry goods business in Oakland in 1879 at 10th and Broadway. He died in 1883. His sons continued the business. Frederick Kahn (1860-1925), Rose's father, worked first at Bauer Brothers Company in San Francisco, then joined the family store. In 1912, Kahn Brothers Department Store moved to a Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway, designed by architect Charles W. Dickey with a 120-foot elliptical dome. The building is now the Rotunda Building, Oakland Landmark #132, listed on the National Register.

Frederick served as first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay (founded 1918), founding member and president of Temple Sinai, chairman of the Oakland Board of Education's Advisory Committee for a $5 million school building program, and vice president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. He died August 19, 1925 at his home at 3933 Harrison Street, Oakland, and was buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

The Lavenson Connection

Samuel Lavenson (1829-1900) and Caroline Heiman (1842-1872) married and had at least six children: Moses, Clara (1870), Sara (1871), Helen (1872), Etta, Albert S. (1865), and George. Caroline died June 24, 1872 in Sacramento at age 29 or 30, the same year her daughter Helen was born. She was buried at Home of Peace Cemetery, Sacramento.

Helen Lavenson (1872-1935) married Frederick Kahn. After Frederick's death in 1925, Helen established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 for 71 paintings, the Kahn Collection of 19th-century California art at the Oakland Museum, catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian. Helen died March 4, 1935 in Oakland.

Albert S. Lavenson (1865-1930), Helen's brother, was partner and vice president of Capwell's Department Store for 30 years starting in 1890. He served as president of Temple Sinai, organizer of the Commercial Club (later Oakland Chamber of Commerce), and music patron at Mills College, responsible for construction of the Music Building. He chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay in 1927. He died at 65 of heart disease and left his estate to a charitable and educational foundation. His daughter Alma Ruth Lavenson (1897-1989) became a noted photographer who studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham.

The Sampson Line: Georgetown and Charleston

Joseph H. Sampson (1823-1883) was a Jewish merchant in Georgetown, South Carolina. Born June 10, 1823 in Charleston, he operated a mercantile business with his brother Samuel. The family originally came from Bury St. Edmunds, England. Georgetown had approximately 80 Jews by 1800, about 10 percent of the white population, making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina. Joseph died July 14, 1883 and was buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen (1832-1897), born June 30, 1832 in Charleston. Hetty was the daughter of Hartwig Cohen (1784-1861), spiritual leader of Charleston's Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim, and Deborah Marks Cohen (1801-1888). Hetty died September 22, 1897 in Georgetown. Her siblings included Sarah Cohen Wolfe (grandmother of financier Bernard Baruch) and Frances A. Cohen.

Joseph and Hetty's son, Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson (1855-1934), was born July 1, 1855 in Georgetown. He attended medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. On April 4, 1887, he married Babette "Bettie" Levy (1868-1949) in Galveston. Bettie was born March 24, 1868 in San Antonio, daughter of Abraham Levy (1827-1879) and Esther Halff (1833-1908). After the 1900 Galveston flood, the family moved to San Francisco in 1902. Their home on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter was dynamited by the U.S. Army as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel. Arthur died November 26, 1934 in San Francisco.

Arthur and Bettie had five children: Joseph Abraham (born December 4, 1888 in Galveston), Arthur Jr. (born June 2, 1891 in Galveston), Cornelia "Nellie" (born August 8, 1893 in Galveston, died 1989 in San Francisco), Esther, and John Jacob (born April 28, 1898 in Galveston).

Rose Etta Kahn and Dr. John Jacob Sampson

Rose Etta Kahn was born December 10, 1907 in Oakland, daughter of Frederick Kahn and Helen Lavenson. She graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. As a girl, she carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication. She became an award-winning weaver, creating furniture upholstery for the family's Seacliff home and a cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. She served as president of the Kahn Foundation and volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into her 80s.

Dr. John Jacob Sampson was born April 28, 1898 in Galveston, Texas. He earned his MD before age 21, attending UC for three years then completing medical school. He served as medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India. He practiced with his father, then joined the UCSF research and clinical staff, where he remained until retirement at 85. He served as Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital.

John published 49 papers between 1926 and 1980 on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman on Type A behavior research and worked with Herman Uhley and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research. The John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology was established at UCSF in his honor.

Rose and John married May 17, 1931. Rose died July 12, 1997 at Mount Zion Hospital of a lung embolism. A memorial service was held August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El. John died December 24, 1987 in San Francisco.

The Sephardic Ancestry Question

Deborah Marks Cohen's mother was Sarah Harris, daughter of Samuel Mendel Marks and granddaughter of Isaac Marks (1732-1776). Isaac Marks appears in multiple genealogical trees as the son of Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, though the connection lacks primary documentation. Isaac fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia and lived in New York City from age 8 in 1740. The family name was anglicized from Marques to Marks.

Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro (1699-1759) first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722 in Barbados. Jacob died around 1725. She then married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro. Isaac Marks was born in 1732 with an unknown father. Esther died July 30, 1759 in Curaçao and was buried at Beth Haim Cemetery, section IV, row C, grave 621. She was the daughter of Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo (1673-1752).

The Levy Maduro family were Sephardic Jews from Amsterdam and Curaçao. Ribi Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro (1649-1708) served as rabbi and cantor, arriving in Curaçao in 1672. His father was Salomon de Moshe Levy Maduro (1629-1699), who married Chana de Castro in Amsterdam on October 27, 1648. Salomon's father was Mosseh Levy o Maduro (circa 1591-1640), who was born in Portugal and adopted the Maduro name. He married Clara Rodriguez on July 27, 1622 in Amsterdam.

The line traces back through Solomon Levy, Salomon (Samuel) Levy, and Antonio Ha Levy Abulafia to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in 15th-century Portugal. However, the connection from Isaac Marks to Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro remains contested. Multiple Geni and Ancestry trees cite this link, but gaps exist in the documentation. DNA testing of Matt Reider showed 99 percent Ashkenazi ancestry. The Sephardic connection remains an open research question.
