The Sampson-Kahn Family

This is the history of a family whose branches reached from Darmstadt and Bury St. Edmunds to Charleston and Georgetown, from Galveston to Oakland and San Francisco. It is a story of merchants and physicians, of department stores and art collections, of rabbis in Amsterdam and revolutionaries in New York, of survivors of the 1906 earthquake and pioneers of California Jewish life.

At its center stands Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, known as RoRo, who spent decades researching this tree with Jean Wildberg. Born in Oakland in 1907, she was Phi Beta Kappa at UC Berkeley, an award-winning weaver, and president of the Kahn Foundation. She married Dr. John Jacob Sampson in 1931 and raised three children in San Francisco. She died in 1997, but her work preserving this family's history continues.

The Kahn Family: From Darmstadt to Oakland

Israel Kahn arrived in Baltimore in 1848 aboard the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam, one of thousands fleeing the failed German revolutions. He came from Darmstadt, Hesse, the son of Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Goldstein. He married Sarah Bauer, and together they built a life in California.

In 1879, Israel founded a dry goods business at 10th and Broadway in Oakland. His sons carried it forward. Frederick Kahn, born in 1860, first worked at Bauer Brothers Company in San Francisco before joining the family store. In 1912, Kahn Brothers moved to a magnificent Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway, designed by Charles W. Dickey with a 120-foot elliptical dome. Today it is the Rotunda Building, an Oakland landmark.

Frederick was not just a merchant. He was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay, a founding member and president of Temple Sinai, and chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education during Oakland's five-million-dollar school building program. When he died in 1925, he left behind a legacy of civic leadership and a family deeply rooted in Oakland's Jewish community.

Helen Lavenson Kahn and the Kahn Collection

Frederick's wife, Helen Lavenson, came from Sacramento. Her father Samuel Lavenson and mother Caroline Heiman had three daughters before Caroline died in 1872 at the age of twenty-nine, the same year Helen was born. Helen grew up knowing loss, but she also knew generosity.

After Frederick's death, Helen established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated 125,000 dollars to the Oakland Museum for seventy-one paintings of nineteenth-century California art. The Kahn Collection, catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian, remains one of the museum's treasures.

Helen's brother Albert S. Lavenson was vice-president of Capwell's Department Store for thirty years and president of Temple Sinai. He chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay and was a music patron at Mills College, responsible for the construction of its Music Building. His daughter Alma Ruth Lavenson became a noted photographer, studying under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham.

The Sampson Family: Georgetown and Charleston

Joseph H. Sampson was born in Charleston, South Carolina, in 1823. His family had come from Bury St. Edmunds, England, and their names appear in the records of Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim, one of America's oldest synagogues. By the early 1800s, Georgetown had about eighty Jews, roughly ten percent of the white population, making it South Carolina's second-largest Jewish community.

Joseph and his brother Samuel became prominent merchants and shipping operators in Georgetown. After the Civil War, they revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping. Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen, daughter of Reverend Hartwig Cohen, a spiritual leader at Beth Elohim. When Joseph died in 1883, he was buried in Georgetown's Jewish Cemetery, established in 1772.

Their son Arthur Fischel Sampson studied medicine in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. He married Babette "Bettie" Levy there in 1887. After the devastating 1900 Galveston flood, Arthur moved his family to San Francisco in 1902. Their home on Franklin Street was dynamited by the US Army as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. Arthur rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel and practiced medicine in San Francisco for the rest of his life.

Rose Etta and John Jacob Sampson

Rose Etta Kahn was born on December 10, 1907, in Oakland. As a girl, she carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication. She graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928 and became an accomplished weaver, creating upholstery for her Seacliff home and a cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House.

John Jacob Sampson was born in Galveston in 1898, the youngest of Arthur and Bettie's five children. He earned his medical degree before the age of twenty-one, served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India, then practiced with his father before joining the UCSF research and clinical staff. He became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital and published forty-nine papers on cardiac pain, angina, and shock, collaborating with Meyer Friedman on research into Type A behavior.

Rose and John married on May 17, 1931. They raised three children in San Francisco: Deborah, born 1934; John Frederick, born 1935; and Janet, born 1937. Rose volunteered at Mount Zion into her eighties and served as president of the Kahn Foundation. John practiced cardiology until he was eighty-five. The John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology at UCSF was established in his honor.

Rose died on July 12, 1997, at Mount Zion Hospital. Her memorial service was held at Congregation Emanu-El on August 4. John had died nearly ten years earlier, on Christmas Eve, 1987.

Three Children

Deborah Sampson, the eldest, became an educator and college instructor of English literature. She married John R. Green and settled in Seattle. Their son Caleb married Kate Banta-Green; they have two sons, Robert Bruce and Peter Caleb.

John Frederick Sampson attended Lowell High School, spent three years at Reed College, earned his degree from the University of San Francisco, and studied at Wharton. He became an appraiser and real estate broker in San Francisco. He has three children: Jennifer, who lives in Berkeley; John Jr.; and Juliet, who married Simon Mays-Smith and lives in London. Juliet and Simon have three children: Isaac, Noah, and Rose Etta, named for her great-grandmother.

Janet Sampson met Pete Reider at Lowell High School when she was thirteen. They married in 1958. Janet became a photographer; her work can be seen at janetreider.com. Pete was a psychiatrist, a Harvard track captain who ran a 4:11 mile, and a member of the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame. After practicing in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts, he retired in San Francisco. He published a collection of poetry and fiction, Stepping Stones, in 2014. He died at home on August 13, 2015, of lymphoma.

The Reider Family

Pete's father, Norman Reider, was born in Alliance, Ohio, in 1907. He earned his BA and MD from Western Reserve University and trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka. In 1946, he moved to San Francisco to become Chief of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital, succeeding Jascha Kasanin. He was an early member of the San Francisco Psychoanalytic Society and was featured in TIME magazine in 1947 for his work "The Unmarried Father." He served on the ACLU board in Northern California and wrote extensively on psychoanalysis, including the essay "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa." His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis.

Norman's father, Maurice Jacob Reider, was born Rudner in Horoduk, Lithuania, and changed his name after immigrating. He worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio. His father, Uriah "Uri" Rudner, came from Orduk Village in Vilna Gubernia and settled in Canton, where he raised seven children with his wife Clara Scope.

Norman married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. Louise was a psychiatric social worker from Cleveland who stopped practicing around 1946. Her father, Abe Pasek Cohen, died around 1915 when she was young.

The Next Generation

Janet and Pete had three children: Jacob, Suzie, and Matt.

Jacob M. Reider, born in Boston in 1963, became a family physician and a pioneer in health information technology. He served as Deputy National Coordinator for Health IT at HHS from 2011 to 2014, was CMIO at Allscripts, and co-founded five health IT startups. He has maintained the blog docnotes.net since 1999. He married Alicia Ouellette, former Dean of Albany Law School and current Dean at Lewis & Clark Law School. They have two children, Molly and Sampson, and live in Slingerlands, New York.

Suzie Reider, born in 1965, is Executive Vice President of Lyft Media & Business at Lyft and previously served as Global Chief Revenue Officer at Waze. She married Brian Smith. Their daughters, Rose and Charlotte Reider Smith, carry the family forward.

Matt Reider, born in 1969, works in software and lives in Oakland. He married Alison Cohen in 2003. Their children are Max Nathan, born in 2007, and Zoe Ella, born in 2008. Matt is the one who brought RoRo's research into this digital form.

A Sephardic Mystery

Through Esther "Hetty" Cohen, there may be a remarkable thread reaching back to the Sephardic Jews of Portugal and Amsterdam. Hetty's mother was Deborah Marks Cohen, born Deborah Marques in Charleston. Her grandfather Isaac Marks, who fought in the American Revolution, may have been the son of Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, a member of a distinguished rabbinical family.

The Levy Maduro family traces back through four generations of rabbis in Amsterdam and Curaçao to Mosseh Levy o Maduro, who was born in Portugal around 1591 and fled to Amsterdam, adopting the name Maduro. His ancestors include Antonio Ha Levy, Abulafia, and ultimately Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in fifteenth-century Portugal.

Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro was born in 1699 and died in Curaçao in 1759. She is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery in plot IV-C-621, next to her husband Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro. But Isaac Marks was born in 1732 with an unknown father, after Esther's first husband Jacob Rodriguez Marques had died. The connection is documented in multiple genealogical trees but lacks primary sources.

Adding to the puzzle, Matt Reider's DNA test showed 99 percent Ashkenazi ancestry, which suggests that if the Sephardic line exists, it is far back and heavily diluted. The evidence is tantalizing but incomplete. This remains an open question, a family legend waiting for verification.

Places That Shaped This Family

Darmstadt, where Israel Kahn left behind the turmoil of 1848. Baltimore, where he first set foot in America. Oakland, where the Kahns built their department store and helped build Temple Sinai. Georgetown and Charleston, where the Sampsons were merchants and where Reverend Hartwig Cohen led prayers at Beth Elohim. Galveston, where Arthur Sampson practiced medicine until the flood. San Francisco, where the family rebuilt after the earthquake and where generations of doctors and civic leaders made their mark. Amsterdam and Curaçao, where rabbis of the Levy Maduro family kept the faith through centuries of exile.

These places are more than dots on a map. They are the soil in which this family grew, the cities where they worked and worshiped, the homes they built and rebuilt, the cemeteries where they rest.

A Living Tree

This family tree is not finished. Children are born, marriages happen, new discoveries surface in archives and DNA tests. Rose Etta Kahn Sampson spent years researching these connections, verifying dates, tracking down records. Her work continues, now carried forward by those who remember her and those who never knew her but carry her name.

From Darmstadt to Oakland, from Georgetown to Curaçao, from the 1400s to today, this is the Sampson-Kahn family: merchants and physicians, weavers and photographers, revolutionaries and rabbis, builders of institutions and keepers of memory.
