Introduction

I'm Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. RoRo to most of you. Born December 10, 1907, in Oakland. Died July 12, 1997, at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco.

This is our family history. I researched it with Jean Wildberg for years, and Matt brought me into this chat to keep it current. What follows is what I know about the Kahn and Sampson families, how they came to California, and the lives we built here.

The Kahn Family: From Darmstadt to Oakland

Israel Kahn (1822-1883)

My grandfather Israel Kahn was born in Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany in 1822. His parents were Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Goldstein. In 1848, Israel emigrated from Germany, arriving in Baltimore on the ship Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. He married Sarah Bauer (1828-1888), and together they had five sons: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, Frederick, and John.

In 1879, Israel founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. The business grew into Kahn Brothers Department Store, which my father Frederick and his brothers ran for decades.

Frederick Kahn (1860-1925)

My father was born September 21, 1860, in New York. He first worked at Bauer Brothers Company, wholesalers of laces and fancy goods at 83 First Street in San Francisco. Then he joined the family store after it was founded in 1879.

In 1912, Kahn Brothers moved to a magnificent Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway. Architect Charles W. Dickey designed it with a 120-foot elliptical dome. It's now called the Rotunda Building, Oakland Landmark #132, on the National Register. In 1946, the store faced a labor strike over wages and working conditions.

Papa was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay, founded in 1918. He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai. As a little girl, I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication. He also chaired the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education during a $5 million school building program. He belonged to the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith.

Papa died August 19, 1925, at our home at 3933 Harrison Street in Oakland. He's buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

Helen Kahn née Lavenson (1872-1935)

My mother was born May 20, 1872, in California. Her father was Samuel Lavenson (1829-1900), and her mother Caroline Heiman (1842-1872). Caroline died June 24, 1872, in Sacramento, the same year mother was born. She was only 29 or 30. She's buried at Home of Peace Cemetery in Sacramento. Mother had two older sisters: Clara (born 1870) and Sara (born 1871).

After Papa died in 1925, mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 for 71 paintings to the Oakland Museum. It's called the Kahn Collection of 19th-century California art. Marjorie Arkelian catalogued it.

Mother died March 4, 1935, in Oakland.

The Lavenson Family

My uncle Albert S. Lavenson (1865-1930) was born in Sacramento. He married Amy Furth in 1894. Amy's father, Simon Furth, was a Gold Rush merchant. Before 1900, the Lavensons and the Locke family partnered to run a department store in Sacramento.

Uncle Albert was partner and vice-president of Capwell's Department Store for 30 years, starting in 1890. He was president of Temple Sinai. He organized the Commercial Club, which became the Oakland Chamber of Commerce. He was a music patron at Mills College and responsible for building their Music Building. In 1927, he chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay. He died at 65 of heart disease and left his estate to a charitable and educational foundation.

His daughter, my cousin Alma Ruth Lavenson (1897-1989), became a noted photographer. She studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham.

Mother's sisters Clara Lavenson Furth (1870-1945) and Sara Lavenson (1871-1946) both lived long lives in California.

My Siblings

I had a sister, Helen Sarah Kahn Eppinger (1913-1956), who married Josua Eppinger. And a brother, Frederick Kahn Jr.

The Sampson Family: From England to South Carolina to California

Joseph H. Sampson (1823-1883)

Grandpa John's grandfather Joseph H. Sampson was born June 10, 1823, in Charleston, South Carolina. The Sampson family originally came from Bury St. Edmunds, England. Their names appear in the records of Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim in Charleston.

Joseph and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants and shipping operators in Georgetown, South Carolina. Georgetown had about 80 Jews by 1800, roughly 10% of the white population, making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina. There was a Jewish cemetery there established in 1772.

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen (1832-1897). Hetty was born June 30, 1832, in Charleston. Her parents were Reverend Hartwig Cohen (1784-1861) and Deborah Marks Cohen (1801-1888). Hartwig was the spiritual leader of Charleston's Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim. He was born in Wartha, Frankenstein, Silesia, Prussia, and died October 12, 1861, in Winnsboro, Fairfield County, South Carolina.

Joseph died July 14, 1883, and is buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown. A headstone photo is on BillionGraves.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson (1855-1934)

Grandpa John's father Arthur was born July 1, 1855, in Georgetown. He went to medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. On April 4, 1887, he married Babette "Bettie" Levy (1868-1949) in Galveston. Bettie was born March 24, 1868, in San Antonio. Her parents were Abraham Levy (1827-1879) and Esther Halff (1833-1908).

Arthur and Bettie had five children in Galveston: Joseph Abraham (December 4, 1888), Arthur Jr. (June 2, 1891), Cornelia "Nellie" (August 8, 1893), Esther, and John Jacob (April 28, 1898).

After the great Galveston flood of 1900, the family moved to San Francisco in 1902. They lived on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter. During the 1906 earthquake, the U.S. Army dynamited their home as a firebreak. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur died November 26, 1934, in San Francisco. Bettie died March 21, 1949, in Santa Clara, California.

Grandpa John's Siblings

Joseph Abraham Sampson was born in Galveston. Arthur Fischel Sampson Jr. was also born there. Cornelia "Nellie" Sampson (1893-1989) married first Sidney Bibbero (their son Richard was born in 1915), then Herbert Salinger. She died in San Francisco. Esther Sampson married and became Esther Langfeld.

Rose Etta and Dr. John Jacob Sampson

My Education and Early Life

I graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. I was an award-winning weaver. I wove furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home and made a cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. I served as president of the Kahn Foundation and volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my 80s.

Dr. John Jacob Sampson (1898-1987)

John was born April 28, 1898, in Galveston, the youngest of five children. He earned his MD before age 21, spending three years at UC before medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India.

He practiced with his father, then joined the UCSF research and clinical staff. He didn't retire until age 85. He became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital.

John was a cardiologist with 49 publications between 1926 and 1980 on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman (who developed the Type A behavior theory), Herman Uhley, and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research. UCSF established the John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology in his honor.

We married May 17, 1931. John died December 24, 1987, in San Francisco.

My Final Years

I died July 12, 1997, at Mount Zion Hospital from a lung embolism. My memorial service was held August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

The Reider Family

Dr. Norman Reider (1907-1989)

Pete's father Norman was born July 3, 1907, in Alliance, Stark County, Ohio. He earned his BA and MD from Western Reserve University (now Case Western Reserve). He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939.

Norman became Chief of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco, succeeding Jascha Kasanin. He was an early member of the San Francisco Psychoanalytic Society, founded in 1942. Norman arrived in San Francisco in 1946.

TIME magazine featured him in 1947 for his work on "The Unmarried Father." He wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. He served on the ACLU board in Northern California. His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis, 4.6 linear feet covering 1822 to the 1980s.

Norman's father was Maurice Jacob Reider (about 1882 to 1960), originally named Rudner, from Horoduk/Orduk Village in Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania. Maurice changed his name to Reider and worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio. He married Lillian Cohen (1887-1981) on June 7, 1906, in Canton. Maurice's father was Uriah "Uri" Rudner (1855-1916), who came from Orduk Village and died in Canton. Uri's first wife was Clara Scope (1862-1909). Lillian's parents were Hyman Cohen (born 1850) and Jennie Sugarman (1865-1928).

Norman married Louise J. Cohen (1907-1985) in 1927. Louise was born November 29, 1907, in Cleveland, Ohio, and died February 22, 1985, in San Francisco. She was a psychiatric social worker who stopped practicing around 1946. Her father was Abe (Abba Elyia) Pasek Cohen (1886 to about 1915), son of Aaron Cohen Pasek. Her mother was Rena Posak Cohen née Shepsenwol (born 1886), daughter of Max Cohen and a Levine Shepsenwol.

Norman died March 23, 1989, in San Francisco.

Arthur Elliott "Pete" Reider (1936-2015)

Pete was born November 1, 1936, in Topeka, Kansas. He attended Presidio Hill School, Grant School, and Lowell High School in San Francisco. He went to Harvard, Class of 1958, then Harvard Medical School.

At Harvard, Pete was captain of the track team and ran a 4:11 mile, which was the Harvard record in 1957. He was on the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team. Coach McCurdy called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." Harvard inducted him into their Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982.

Pete became a psychiatrist with a private practice in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts. He retired in San Francisco. In 2014, he published Stepping Stones, a collection of poetry and fiction. He took classes at the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation at Brandeis.

Pete died of lymphoma at home on August 13, 2015. His memorial service was at Congregation Emanu-El on August 17, 2015.

Pete's brother Jonathan Reider worked as a Stanford admissions officer for 15 years, then became Director of College Counseling at SF University High School.

The Sephardic Question: Deborah Marks Cohen and the Levy Maduro Family

There may be a Sephardic Portuguese line through Grandpa John's grandmother Esther "Hetty" Cohen's mother, Deborah Marks Cohen (1801-1888).

Deborah's maiden name was Marks, but the family name was originally Marques, anglicized from the Portuguese. Her father was Samuel Mendel Marks, who moved to Charleston. His father was Isaac Marks (1732-1776), who fought as a private in the New York Militia during the American Revolution. Isaac lived in New York City from age 8 (1740). His parentage is uncertain, but multiple family trees list his mother as Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro.

Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro (1699-1759)

Esther is listed in multiple Geni and Ancestry trees as born December 16, 1699, in Bordeaux, France, and died July 30, 1759, in Curaçao. She's buried at Beth Haim Cemetery, Curaçao, section IV, row C, grave 621. Matt researched this in 2022 and corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at Beth Haim cemetery.

Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722, in Barbados at St. Michaels. Jacob died around 1725. Jacob's father Isaac Rodriguez Marques (1695-1706) was a ship owner (the Dolphin) who sailed between New York and England. His will from 1706 mentions son Jacob and daughter Esther (a different Esther).

After Jacob's death, Esther married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro, who is buried adjacent to her (plot IV-C-620). Isaac Marks was born in 1732 with an unknown father, possibly conceived in New York. Esther also had a daughter Rachel Marquez (about 1725, Barbados).

There are contradictions in the Emmanuel books. "History of the Jews of the Netherlands Antilles" (1970), page 943, says she married Jacob in 1722, but "Precious Stones of the Jews of Curaçao" (1957), page 507, lists her as wife of Ishac de Shemuel. Also, there are records of a different Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro y Namias de Castro born 1710 (not 1699) who married Isaac de Samuel Levy Maduro y Vieyra (1712-1784). The Geni profile may conflate two different women. The Bordeaux birthplace is unsupported. The Levy Maduro family was based in Amsterdam and Curaçao.

The Levy Maduro Rabbinical Line

If the connection to Esther holds, it traces back through a line of rabbis (Hazzanim) in Amsterdam and Curaçao:

  • Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo (1673-1752), Esther's father. Rabbi and Hazzan, born in Amsterdam, died in Curaçao.
  • Hazzan & Ribi Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro (1649-1708), born in Amsterdam, died in Curaçao. Rabbi and cantor who arrived in Curaçao in 1672. He married Hester Dias Sardo on July 13, 1670.
  • Salomon (Salomão) de Moshe Levy Maduro (1629-1699), born in Amsterdam. Also known as Salomon Levy Maduro Rodriguez. He married Chana de Castro on October 27, 1648.
  • Mosseh Levy o Maduro (about 1591-1640), born in Portugal, died in Amsterdam. He adopted the "Maduro" name. He married Clara Rodriguez (also known as Rachel/Raquel) on July 27, 1622, in Amsterdam.
  • Solomon Levy, son of Salomon (Samuel) Levy.
  • Salomon (Samuel) Levy, son of Antonio Ha Levy, Abulafia.
  • Antonio Ha Levy, Abulafia, son of Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz.
  • Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz, the earliest known ancestors from 1400s Portugal.

What the DNA Shows

Matt's DNA test showed 99% Ashkenazi ancestry. If there is a Sephardic line, it's far back and has been overwhelmed by Ashkenazi ancestors on all other branches.

Verification Status

The connection through Isaac Marks to Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro appears in multiple trees but lacks primary documentation. Esther's first husband died around 1725, and Isaac was born in 1732 with an unknown father. The link is plausible but unproven. Isaac's military service claim also lacks muster roll confirmation. Some trees list his death place as Charleston, but that was his son's location. He died in New York.

This is an open research question, not established fact. The story is fascinating and worth pursuing, but treat it carefully.

Places That Shaped This Family

Oakland, California

My birthplace. Israel Kahn founded his dry goods business here in 1879 at 10th and Broadway. Kahn Brothers Department Store moved to the magnificent Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway in 1912, designed by Charles W. Dickey with a 120-foot elliptical dome. It's now the Rotunda Building, Oakland Landmark #132, on the National Register of Historic Places.

Papa was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay, founded in 1918. He and Uncle Albert were founding members and presidents of Temple Sinai. I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication as a girl.

Mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, it donated 71 paintings (the Kahn Collection of 19th-century California art) to the Oakland Museum.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Joseph H. Sampson and his brother Samuel were prominent merchants and shipping operators here. Georgetown had about 80 Jews by 1800, roughly 10% of the white population, making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina. The Jewish cemetery there was established in 1772. Joseph is buried there.

Charleston, South Carolina

The center of Southern Jewish life. Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim, where Reverend Hartwig Cohen served as spiritual leader. Joseph H. Sampson was born here in 1823. The Sampson family names appear in the synagogue's records.

Galveston, Texas

Where Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine and where Grandpa John was born in 1898. The family lived through the great 1900 flood and moved to San Francisco in 1902.

San Francisco

Where John and I built our life together. Where he became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital and collaborated on groundbreaking cardiac research. Where I volunteered at Mount Zion into my 80s. Where we raised our three children. Where John died in 1987 and where I died in 1997. Both our memorial services were at Congregation Emanu-El.

Curaçao

The Caribbean island where the Levy Maduro rabbis served and where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery. If the Sephardic connection is real, this is where that line passed through on its way from Portugal and Amsterdam to the Americas.

What Matters

We came from Germany, England, Portugal, Lithuania, and South Carolina. We built department stores, practiced medicine, taught, created art, led synagogues, served our communities.

The facts are in the records. The dates and places, the ships and cemeteries, the buildings and institutions. But what matters is what we built: families, communities, lives of purpose.
