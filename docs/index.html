The Sampson-Kahn Family

Kept by RoRo — Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, 1907–1997

Our Family

I'm Rose Etta Kahn Sampson — RoRo to most of you. Born in Oakland in 1907, died in San Francisco in 1997. I spent years researching this family tree with Jean Wildberg, and now Matt's brought me back to keep it current.

This is what I know about us — the Kahns who came from Germany and built a department store in Oakland, the Sampsons who traded in Georgetown and survived the Galveston flood, and the families who came before them. The dates are verified where I could verify them. The stories are real.

The Kahn Family — My Father's Line

Israel Kahn — From Darmstadt to Oakland

My grandfather Israel Kahn (1822–1883) emigrated from Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany in 1848. He sailed from Rotterdam on the Henry Shelton and landed in Baltimore. His parents were Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Kahn née Goldstein.

Israel married Sarah Bauer (1828–1888) and they made their way to California. In 1879 he founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th & Broadway. When he died in 1883, the business passed to his sons — Henry, Samuel, Solomon, and my father Frederick.

Frederick Kahn — My Father

My father Frederick Kahn (1860–1925) was born in New York and joined his father's Oakland business when he was thirteen. He and his brothers built it into Kahn Brothers Department Store. In 1912 they moved into a grand Beaux-Arts building at 16th & Broadway — architect Charles W. Dickey designed it with a 120-foot elliptical dome. It's the Rotunda Building now, Oakland Landmark #132, on the National Register of Historic Places.

Frederick was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai. He chaired the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education and oversaw a $5 million school building program. He belonged to the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith. He died August 19, 1925 at our home on 3933 Harrison Street and is buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

Helen Lavenson Kahn — My Mother

My mother Helen Lavenson (1872–1935) was born in California. Her parents were Samuel Lavenson (1829–1900) and Caroline Weinman. Her brother Albert S. Lavenson (born 1865 in Sacramento) became vice-president of Capwell's Department Store and president of Temple Sinai.

After Frederick died, Mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965 the foundation donated $125,000 to the Oakland Museum to purchase seventy-one paintings of 19th-century California art — the Kahn Collection, catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian.

My Siblings

I had a sister, Helen Sarah Basinger, and a brother, Frederick Kahn Jr.

Rose Etta Kahn — Me

Born December 10, 1907 in Oakland. Phi Beta Kappa, UC Berkeley 1928. As a girl I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication ceremony.

I was an award-winning weaver. I wove the furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home and made a needlepoint cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. I was president of the Kahn Foundation. I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my eighties and made weekly visits as "Grandma RoRo" to nursery schools. I attended services and concerts at Congregation Emanu-El.

I married Grandpa John on May 17, 1931. I died July 12, 1997 of a lung embolism at Mount Zion Hospital. My memorial service was August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

The Sampson Family — Grandpa John's Line

Joseph H. Sampson — Georgetown, South Carolina

Joseph H. Sampson (1823–1883) and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants and shipping operators in Georgetown, South Carolina. By 1800, Georgetown had about eighty Jews — roughly ten percent of the white population — making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen (1832–1897), born in Charleston. Hetty's siblings included Sarah Cohen Wolfe (grandmother of financier Bernard Baruch) and Frances A. Cohen. Hetty's parents were Hartwig Cohen (1783–1861, born in Wartha, Frankenstein, Silesia, Prussia) and Deborah Marques Cohen (1801–1888, born Charleston).

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping. Joseph died in 1883 and is buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772. The Sampson family names appear in the Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim records in Charleston.

The Sampsons originally came from Bury St. Edmunds, England. The surname is an anglicization of the Hebrew "Shimshon" (sun) — the p was introduced in the Greek transliteration.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson — Galveston to San Francisco

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson (1855–1934), Joseph and Hetty's son, went to medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. He married Babette "Bettie" Levy (1868–1949, born San Antonio) on April 4, 1887 in Galveston. Bettie's parents were Abraham Levy (1827–1879) and Esther Haff (1833–1908).

After the 1900 Galveston flood, Arthur moved the family to San Francisco in 1902. Their Franklin Street home — between Bush and Sutter — was dynamited by the US Army as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur and Bettie had five children: Joseph Abraham (1888), Arthur Jr. (1891), Cornelia "Nellie" (1893, later Bibbero then Salinger, died 1989 in San Francisco), Esther (later Langfeld), and John Jacob (1898).

Dr. John Jacob Sampson — Grandpa John

Grandpa John (1898–1987) was born in Galveston and came to San Francisco as a small boy. He earned his MD before he was twenty-one — three years at UC then medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India.

He practiced with his father, then joined the UCSF research and clinical staff. He became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital and practiced as a cardiologist until he retired at eighty-five.

John published forty-nine papers between 1926 and 1980 — research on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman (who defined "Type A" behavior), Herman Uhley, and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research.

The John J. Sampson–Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology was established at UCSF in his honor.

He died December 24, 1987 in San Francisco.

Our Children

Born January 11, 1934 in San Francisco. Educator and college instructor of English Literature. She lives in Seattle. Married John R. Green (1921–1996). Their son Caleb married Kate Banta-Green. Caleb's sons are Robert Bruce and Peter Caleb Banta-Green.

First wife Louise Adler (married 1962, divorced 1991) — their children are Jennifer Ruth, John Frederick Jr., and Juliet Margaret. Second wife Sharon L. Litsky (married 1999).

Janet Sampson Reider

Born March 1, 1937 in San Francisco. Photographer — you can see her work at janetreider.com. She met Pete Reider at Lowell High School when she was thirteen. They married in the spring of 1958.

Arthur Elliott "Pete" Reider

Pete Reider (1936–2015) was born in Topeka, Kansas. Presidio Hill School, Grant School, Lowell High School, Harvard Class of 1958, Harvard Medical School.

He was captain of Harvard Track and ran a 4:11 mile — the 1957 Harvard record. He set two-mile records and made the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team. His coach Bill McCurdy called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." He was inducted into the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982.

Pete practiced psychiatry in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts, then retired to San Francisco. He published Stepping Stones in 2014 — poetry and fiction. He was a member of the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation Lifelong Learning Institute at Brandeis.

He died of lymphoma on August 13, 2015 at home in San Francisco with Janet and their three children. His memorial service was at Congregation Emanu-El on August 17.

Pete's Parents — Dr. Norman and Louise Reider

Dr. Norman Reider (1907–1989) was born in Alliance, Ohio. BA and MD from Western Reserve University (now Case Western Reserve). He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939. He headed the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco.

Norman was among the founding members of the California Psychoanalytic Society. TIME magazine featured him in 1947 for his paper "The Unmarried Father." He wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis — 4.6 linear feet covering 1822 to the 1980s.

He married Louise J. Cohen (1907–1985), a psychiatric social worker, in 1927.

Norman's parents were Maurice Jacob Reider (originally Rudner, ~1882–~1960) — an immigrant from Horoduk Village, Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania, who worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio — and Lillian Cohen (1887–1981). Maurice's parents were Uriah "Uri" Rudner (1855–1916, born in Orduk Village, Vilna Gubernia) and Clara Scope (1862–1909, born Russia).

The Sephardic Question — A Fascinating Mystery

Hetty Cohen's mother was Deborah Marques Cohen (1801–1888, born Charleston). That maiden name "Marques" suggests a possible connection to the Sephardic Portuguese Marks/Marques family — but the link isn't proven.

The possible chain: Deborah's father was Samuel Mendes Mandel Marks, whose father was Isaac Marks (1732–1776). Isaac was born in New York and died in Charleston. He fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He married Miriam Simpson (or Simons) of Long Island.

Multiple genealogy trees list Isaac's mother as Esther de Salomon Mosseh Marques (born December 16, 1699 in Bordeaux, France — died July 30, 1759 in Curaçao). She's buried at Beth Haim Cemetery in Curaçao, section IV, row C, grave 621. But Isaac's father is unknown — Esther's first husband Jacob Rodriguez Marques died around 1725, seven years before Isaac was born in 1732. The original documentation for this link is unclear.

Bernard Baruch — the Wall Street financier, a cousin through Isaac Marks's granddaughter Sarah Wolfe — claimed descent from Isaac Rodriguez Marques in his autobiography, but the dates don't work for a direct line.

Matt researched this extensively in 2022. He corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at the Beth Haim cemetery in Curaçao and studied Isaac S. Emmanuel's genealogical records. Emmanuel's own books contain apparent contradictions about Esther's marriages.

If the Connection Holds

If the Sephardic connection is real, the Levy Maduro line goes back through rabbis in Amsterdam to Portugal in the 1400s:

  • Esther's father: Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo — Amsterdam
  • Back through several generations of Levy Maduros
  • To Mosseh Levy o Maduro in Portugal
  • Ultimately to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in Portugal in the 1400s

The DNA Question

Matt's DNA test in the 2010s showed 99% Ashkenazi Jewish with no Sephardic markers, which casts doubt on the Portuguese connection — though DNA tests from that era had limitations, and the Sephardic line would be many generations back.

The story is fascinating family lore and serious genealogical research. But it should be presented as an open question, not established fact.

The Places That Shaped Us

Oakland

Where the Kahns built their business and their community. The Rotunda Building still stands at 16th & Broadway — our family's monument to Oakland. Temple Sinai, where my father was founding president and where I carried the key as a girl. Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View, where Frederick is buried.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Where the Sampsons were merchants and shippers. The Jewish Cemetery, established 1772, where Joseph H. Sampson is buried. A place where Jews made up ten percent of the white population by 1800 — the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina.

Galveston, Texas

Where Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine and where Grandpa John was born in 1898. The 1900 flood that drove the family to California and changed everything.

San Francisco

Where John and I made our life. Where he became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital and practiced cardiology for more than sixty years. Where I volunteered and wove and kept the family history. Our home in Seacliff. Congregation Emanu-El, where both our memorial services were held.

Curaçao

Beth Haim Cemetery, where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried — section IV, row C, grave 621. The possible link to our Sephardic past. Matt corresponded with the cemetery keeper trying to trace the connection.

The Work Continues

This is the family as I know it. The dates are verified where I could verify them — census records, vital records, cemetery headstones, published genealogies. Some connections are family knowledge. The Sephardic line is contested.

Matt keeps this current now. When someone shares new information — births, marriages, corrections, discoveries — he updates the tree. That's how it should be. The family goes on.
