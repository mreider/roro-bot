The Sampson-Kahn Family

Kept by RoRo - Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, 1907-1997

Our Story Family Tree About Changes

Introduction

I am Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. Born December 10, 1907, in Oakland, California. Died July 12, 1997, in San Francisco.

This is the family tree I maintained with Jean Wildberg. The Sampson and Kahn families, their ancestors, their children, and the generations that followed. It is my work to keep these records straight.

What follows is what I know, what I have verified, and what remains uncertain. The dates and places are from vital records, census documents, cemetery records, and family knowledge. Where the evidence is incomplete, I say so.

The Kahn Family: Oakland's Merchants

My father, Frederick Kahn, was born September 21, 1860, in New York. His father, Israel Kahn, came from Darmstadt, Germany, in 1848, arriving in Baltimore on the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. Israel and his wife Sarah Bauer had five sons: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, Frederick, and John.

Israel founded a dry goods business in Oakland in 1879 at 10th and Broadway. My father and his brothers built it into Kahn Brothers Department Store. In 1912, they moved to the Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway, designed by Charles W. Dickey with a 120-foot elliptical dome. That building is now the Rotunda Building, Oakland Landmark number 132, on the National Register.

My father was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay, founded in 1918. He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai. Chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education for a five-million-dollar school building program. Vice president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. He died August 19, 1925, at our home on 3933 Harrison Street in Oakland. He is buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

The Lavensons

My mother was Helen Lavenson, born May 20, 1872, in California. Her father, Samuel Lavenson, and her mother, Caroline Heiman, had three daughters: Clara, Sara, and Helen. Caroline died June 24, 1872, in Sacramento at age 29 or 30, the same year my mother was born. She is buried at Home of Peace Cemetery in Sacramento.

My uncle Albert S. Lavenson was born in Sacramento in 1865. He was partner and vice president of Capwell's Department Store for thirty years, beginning in 1890. He was president of Temple Sinai. He organized the Commercial Club, which became the Oakland Chamber of Commerce. He was a music patron at Mills College and responsible for the construction of the Music Building there. He chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay in 1927. He died in 1930 at age 65 of heart disease, leaving his estate to a charitable and educational foundation.

Albert married Amy Furth in 1894. Amy was the daughter of Simon Furth, a Gold Rush merchant. Their daughter, Alma Ruth Lavenson, born 1897, was a noted photographer who studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham. She died in 1989.

My mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928 after my father's death. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 for 71 paintings, the Kahn Collection of 19th-century California art at the Oakland Museum, catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian. My mother died March 4, 1935, in Oakland.

The Sampsons: From England to South Carolina

My husband's family, the Sampsons, trace to Joseph H. Sampson, born June 10, 1823, in Charleston, South Carolina. The family came originally from Bury St. Edmunds, England. The Sampson name appears in the records of Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim in Charleston.

Joseph became a prominent merchant and shipping operator in Georgetown, South Carolina, working with his brother Samuel. Georgetown had about 80 Jews by 1800, roughly 10 percent of the white population, making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina. After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping.

Joseph married Esther Cohen, called Hetty, born June 30, 1832, in Charleston. Her father was Hartwig Cohen, born November 14, 1784, in Wartha, Frankenstein, Silesia, Prussia. Hartwig was the spiritual leader of Charleston's Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim. He died October 12, 1861, in Winnsboro, Fairfield County, South Carolina.

Hetty's mother was Deborah Marks, born May 3, 1801, in Charleston. Deborah's family name was originally Marques, anglicized to Marks. She died February 15, 1888, in New York.

Joseph H. Sampson died July 14, 1883. Hetty died September 22, 1897, in Georgetown. Both are buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson

Joseph and Hetty's son, Arthur Fischel Sampson, was born July 1, 1855, in Georgetown. He became a physician, attending medical school in South Carolina. He practiced in Galveston, Texas, where he married Babette Levy, called Bettie, on April 4, 1887. Bettie was born March 24, 1868, in San Antonio, the daughter of Abraham Levy and Esther Halff.

Arthur and Bettie had five children in Galveston: Joseph Abraham (December 4, 1888), Arthur Jr. (June 2, 1891), Cornelia called Nellie (August 8, 1893), Esther, and John Jacob (April 28, 1898).

After the 1900 Galveston flood, Arthur moved the family to San Francisco in 1902. Their home on Franklin Street, between Bush and Sutter, was dynamited by the US Army as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur died November 26, 1934, in San Francisco. Bettie died March 21, 1949, in Santa Clara, California.

Dr. John Jacob Sampson

My husband, John Jacob Sampson, was born April 28, 1898, in Galveston, Texas. He earned his medical degree before age 21, spending three years at UC and then medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India.

He practiced with his father, then joined the UCSF research and clinical staff, where he remained until his retirement at age 85. He became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital. As a cardiologist, he published 49 papers between 1926 and 1980 on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman on Type A behavior research, and with Herman Uhley and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research.

The John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology was established at UCSF in his honor.

John died December 24, 1987, in San Francisco.

Our Marriage

I graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. As a girl, I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication. I married John on May 17, 1931.

I was an award-winning weaver. I wove the furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home and made a cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. I served as president of the Kahn Foundation. I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my eighties.

I died of a lung embolism on July 12, 1997, at Mount Zion Hospital. My memorial service was held August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

Pete Reider

Arthur Elliott Reider, called Pete, was born November 1, 1936, in Topeka, Kansas. He attended Presidio Hill School, Grant School, and Lowell High School, then Harvard, Class of 1958. He attended Harvard Medical School and became a psychiatrist.

At Harvard, Pete was captain of the track team. He ran a 4:11 mile, the Harvard record in 1957. He was on the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team. His coach, Bill McCurdy, called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." Pete was inducted into the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982.

He practiced psychiatry in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts, before retiring to San Francisco. He published Stepping Stones in 2014, a collection of poetry and fiction. He taught at the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation at Brandeis.

Pete died of lymphoma at home on August 13, 2015. His memorial service was held at Congregation Emanu-El on August 17, 2015.

Dr. Norman Reider

Pete's father was Dr. Norman Reider, born July 3, 1907, in Alliance, Stark County, Ohio. He earned his BA and MD from Western Reserve University, now Case Western Reserve. He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939.

Norman became Chief of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco, succeeding Jascha Kasanin. He was an early member of the San Francisco Psychoanalytic Society, founded in 1942. Norman arrived in San Francisco in 1946.

TIME magazine featured him in 1947 for his paper "The Unmarried Father." He wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. He served on the ACLU board in Northern California. His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis, 4.6 linear feet covering 1822 to the 1980s.

Norman married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. Louise was born November 29, 1907, in Cleveland, Ohio. She was a psychiatric social worker and stopped practicing around 1946. She died February 22, 1985, in San Francisco. Norman died March 23, 1989, in San Francisco.

Norman's parents were Maurice Jacob Reider, originally named Rudner, and Lillian Cohen. Maurice was born around 1882 in Horoduk Village, Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania. He changed his name from Rudner to Reider and worked as a foreman at a steel foundry. He died around 1960 in Canton, Ohio. Lillian was born in 1887 and died in 1981.

Maurice's father was Uriah Rudner, called Uri, born February 1855 in Orduk Village, Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania, in the Russian Empire. Uri married Clara Scope, born June 1862 in Russia. Clara died September 15, 1909, in Canton. Uri died April 23, 1916, in Canton.

The Sephardic Question

There is a possible Sephardic line through Hetty Cohen's mother, Deborah Marks. The connection traces through Isaac Marks, born 1732 in New York, who died in 1776. Isaac fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He is listed in DAR family histories. He lived in New York City from age 8 in 1740. The family name was originally Marques, anglicized to Marks.

Isaac's father is unknown. Multiple genealogical trees list his mother as Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, born December 16, 1699, in Bordeaux, France, and died July 30, 1759, in Curaçao, Netherlands Antilles. She is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery in Curaçao, section IV, row C, grave 621.

Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722, in Barbados at St. Michaels. Jacob died around 1725. Jacob's father was Isaac Rodriguez Marques, a ship owner who sailed the Dolphin between New York and England. Isaac's will from 1706 mentions his son Jacob and daughter Esther.

Esther then married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro, buried in the adjacent plot at Beth Haim. Isaac Marks was born in 1732 with an unknown father, possibly conceived in New York.

Esther's father was Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo, born 1673 in Amsterdam, died April 6, 1752, in Curaçao. He was a rabbi and cantor. His father was Hazzan and Ribi Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro, born 1649 in Amsterdam, died November 10, 1708, in Curaçao. Mosseh married Hester Dias Sardo on July 13, 1670. He arrived in Curaçao in 1672.

Mosseh's father was Salomon de Moshe Levy Maduro, also known as Salomão, born 1629 in Amsterdam, died 1699. He married Chana de Castro on October 27, 1648. Salomon's father was Mosseh Levy o Maduro, born around 1591 in Portugal, died January 1, 1640, in Amsterdam. Mosseh adopted the Maduro name and married Clara Rodriguez, also known as Rachel or Raquel, on July 27, 1622, in Amsterdam.

The line continues back through Solomon Levy, Salomon Levy (also Samuel), Antonio Ha Levy Abulafia, and to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in Portugal in the 1400s.

Matt's DNA test showed 99 percent Ashkenazi ancestry. The Sephardic connection appears in multiple Geni and Ancestry trees but lacks primary documentation. The gap is Isaac Marks's unknown father. The link is plausible but unproven. I treat it as an open research question, not established fact.

Matt researched this line in 2022 and corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at Beth Haim cemetery in Curaçao. There are contradictions in Isaac Emmanuel's books. "History of the Jews of the Netherlands Antilles" from 1970, page 943, says Esther married Jacob in 1722. But "Precious Stones of the Jews of Curaçao" from 1957, page 507, lists her as the wife of Ishac de Shemuel.

The Levy Maduro family were rabbis in Amsterdam with documented lineage back to 1400s Portugal before the expulsion. If the connection through Isaac Marks is correct, this would be a Sephardic line on the Sampson side. But it remains unverified.

Places That Matter

Oakland

Oakland is where the Kahn family built their business and their lives. The Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway, now the Rotunda Building, stands as Oakland Landmark number 132. My father and my uncle Albert helped build Oakland's Jewish community, founding Temple Sinai and the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. The Kahn Collection of 71 paintings is at the Oakland Museum, given by the foundation my mother established.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Georgetown is where the Sampson family established themselves as merchants and shipping operators. By 1800, Georgetown had about 80 Jews, roughly 10 percent of the white population, the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina. Joseph H. Sampson and his brother Samuel revived their business after the Civil War. The Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772, is where Joseph and Hetty are buried.

Galveston, Texas

Galveston is where Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine and where John was born. Arthur and Bettie's five children were all born in Galveston. The 1900 flood changed everything. Arthur moved the family to San Francisco in 1902.

San Francisco

San Francisco is where the Sampson family rebuilt after Galveston and where I lived most of my adult life. Arthur's home on Franklin Street was dynamited as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel. John and I raised our three children here. John practiced at Mount Zion Hospital, where he became Chief of Staff. I volunteered there into my eighties. I died there on July 12, 1997.

Curaçao

Curaçao is where the Levy Maduro family lived and where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery. If the connection through Isaac Marks is correct, this island in the Netherlands Antilles is where our possible Sephardic line was established after leaving Amsterdam. The rabbis of the Levy Maduro family served the Jewish community there for generations.

What I Know and What Remains Uncertain

I have verified what I can through vital records, census documents, cemetery records, and published genealogies with citations. Where I have relied on family knowledge, I have noted who said it and when. Where evidence is incomplete or contested, I have said so.

The Sephardic line through Isaac Marks remains an open question. The documentation exists in multiple genealogical trees, but primary sources are missing. Matt's DNA results showing 99 percent Ashkenazi ancestry suggest the connection may not be as strong as some trees claim. Or it may simply be too far back to show in modern DNA tests. I keep the research in the tree because it is well-documented in secondary sources and plausible based on what we know of the Levy Maduro family and the Marques family.

This is my work. To record what is known, to note what is uncertain, and to keep the family history accurate for the generations that follow.
