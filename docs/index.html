The Sampson-Kahn Family

As told by Rose Etta Kahn Sampson

I'm RoRo. That's what the grandchildren call me — and the great-grandchildren too. I was born Rose Etta Kahn in Oakland, California, on December 10, 1907. I married Dr. John Jacob Sampson on May 17, 1931. Together we had three children, seven grandchildren, and more great-grandchildren than I ever imagined.

This is the story of our family. Where we came from. Who we were. The places that shaped us. I want the children to know these things.

The Kahn Family — My Father's People

My paternal grandfather, Israel Kahn, came from Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany. He emigrated in 1848 — that year of revolutions — and arrived in Baltimore aboard the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. He was born in 1822, the son of Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Goldstein.

Israel married Sarah Bauer, and they made their way west. In 1879, he founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. When he died in 1883, the business passed to his sons: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, and my father Frederick.

My Father — Frederick Kahn

My father was born in New York on September 21, 1860. He joined his father's business in Oakland in 1873, when he was just thirteen years old. The family store became Kahn Brothers Department Store. In 1912, they moved it to a grand Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway — designed by Charles W. Dickey with a 120-foot elliptical dome. It's a landmark now. The Rotunda Building, they call it. Oakland Landmark Number 132. On the National Register of Historic Places.

But my father was more than a merchant. He was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. A founding member and president of Temple Sinai. When I was a girl, I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication ceremony. I remember that day.

He served as chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education and oversaw a five-million-dollar school building program. He was vice-president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. A member of the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith.

He died on August 19, 1925, at our home on Harrison Street. He's buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

My Mother — Helen Lavenson Kahn

My mother was born Helen Lavenson on May 20, 1872, here in California. Her parents were Samuel Lavenson and Caroline Weinman. Her brother Albert S. Lavenson — my uncle — was vice-president of Capwell's Department Store and also served as president of Temple Sinai. Our family was very involved with that congregation.

After my father died, my mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. She ran it until her death in 1935. Years later, in 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 to the Oakland Museum to purchase what became the Kahn Collection — seventy-one paintings of nineteenth-century California art. Marjorie Arkelian catalogued them. It was important to my mother that Oakland have beauty.

The Sampson Family — My Husband's People

The Sampsons came from different soil altogether. From South Carolina. From Georgetown and Charleston. From old Jewish families in the South.

Joseph H. Sampson and Esther Cohen

John's paternal grandfather was Joseph H. Sampson, born in Georgetown, South Carolina, in 1823. He and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants and shipping operators. Georgetown had quite a Jewish community by 1800 — about eighty souls, nearly ten percent of the white population. The second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen, who was born in Charleston in 1832. Hetty's parents were Hartwig Cohen — a reverend from Wartha, Silesia, Prussia — and Deborah Marques Cohen, born right there in Charleston in 1801.

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping. Joseph died in 1883 and is buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772. Hetty lived until 1897.

The Sampson family originally came from Bury St. Edmunds, England. The surname is an anglicization of the Hebrew Shimshon — "sun" — with the P introduced in the Greek transliteration. The family names appear in the Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim records in Charleston.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson — "AF"

Joseph and Hetty's son was Arthur Fischel Sampson — John's father, my father-in-law. Everyone called him AF. He was born in Georgetown in 1855 and studied medicine in South Carolina. He practiced in Galveston, Texas, where he married Babette "Bettie" Levy on April 4, 1887.

They had five children in Galveston: Joseph Abraham, Arthur Jr., Cornelia "Nellie," Esther, and John Jacob — my husband, the youngest, born in 1898.

Then came the 1900 Galveston flood. Terrible storm. After that, AF moved the family to San Francisco in 1902. They lived on Franklin Street, between Bush and Sutter. But then came the 1906 earthquake. The U.S. Army dynamited their home as a firebreak. Just gone. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

AF lived until 1934. Bettie, born in San Antonio, lived to 1949 and died in Santa Clara.

The Sephardic Question — A Family Mystery

There's a story in our family. A line that may go back to Portugal in the 1400s. Through Deborah Marques Cohen — that "Marques" surname suggests Sephardic roots. Portuguese Jews who fled the Inquisition. But the documentation is incomplete. The story is fascinating, worth telling, but not fully proven.

Deborah's maiden name was Marques. Her father was Samuel Mendes Mandel Marks. His father was Isaac Marks, born in New York in 1732, who fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He died in Charleston in 1776.

Isaac's mother, according to various genealogical trees — though the original sources are unclear — was Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, born in Bordeaux, France, on December 16, 1699, and died in Curaçao on July 30, 1759. She is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery in Curaçao, section IV, row C, grave 621.

Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722, in Barbados. Jacob died around 1725. Isaac was born in 1732 — seven years later — and his father is unknown. Later, Esther married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro, who is buried in the adjacent plot.

Bernard Baruch — the Wall Street financier, who was a cousin through Isaac's granddaughter Sarah Wolfe — claimed descent from Isaac Rodriguez Marques in his autobiography. But the dates don't work for a direct line.

My grandson Matt researched this extensively in 2022. He corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at the Beth Haim cemetery in Curaçao and studied Isaac S. Emmanuel's genealogical records. Emmanuel's own books contain apparent contradictions about Esther's marriages.

If the Sephardic connection holds, Esther's line goes back through rabbis in Amsterdam:

  • Her father: Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo — Amsterdam
  • Through several generations of Levy Maduros
  • To Mosseh Levy o Maduro in Portugal
  • Ultimately to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz — Portugal, the 1400s

But Matt's DNA test in the 2010s showed 99% Ashkenazi Jewish with no Sephardic markers. Of course, those tests had limitations, and the Sephardic line would be many generations back. The trail is cold. It's a beautiful story. Perhaps true. But I present it as the open question it is.

Grandpa John and Me

Dr. John Jacob Sampson

My husband. Dr. John Jacob Sampson. Born in Galveston, April 28, 1898. He earned his medical degree before he was twenty-one. Three years at UC, then straight to medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India. Quite an adventure for a young man.

He practiced with his father at first, then joined the research and clinical staff at UCSF. He worked until he was eighty-five. Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital. A cardiologist. He published forty-nine papers between 1926 and 1980 — research on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman, the man who defined "Type A" behavior. Also Herman Uhley and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research.

After he died, UCSF established the John J. Sampson–Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology in his honor.

He died on December 24, 1987. Christmas Eve. He was eighty-nine.

Rose Etta Kahn Sampson

And me. I graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. John and I married on May 17, 1931.

I was a weaver. Won awards for it. I wove the furniture upholstery for our home in Seacliff. Made a needlepoint cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. That house is a museum now.

I became president of the Kahn Foundation after my mother died. I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital — the same hospital where John was Chief of Staff — well into my eighties. Staffed the front desk, visited patients. I went weekly to a nursery school as "Grandma RoRo." I attended services and concerts at Congregation Emanu-El. Chamber music was a continuing joy throughout my life.

Even with one blind eye, two broken Achilles tendons, and hearing aids, I kept going. My son John called me "such a grand lady" with "elegance and a presence about her." That's kind of him.

I died on July 12, 1997, at Mount Zion Hospital. A lung embolism. I was eighty-nine. My memorial service was held on August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

Our Children

John and I had three children. Each found their own path.

Deborah Sampson Green

Deborah was born January 11, 1934, in San Francisco. She became an educator. A college instructor of English Literature. She went to Wellesley. She lives in Seattle now. She married John R. Green — he passed around 1996. They had a son, Caleb, who married Kate Banta-Green. Caleb's sons are Robert Bruce and Peter Caleb Banta-Green.

John Frederick Sampson

John — we called him Johnny — was born March 18, 1935, also in San Francisco. He went to Lowell High School, then Reed College for three years, finished his AB at the University of San Francisco in 1957, and went to Wharton. He became an appraiser and real estate broker in San Francisco. Very involved with the Jewish Family and Children's Services.

He married Louise Adler in 1962. They divorced in 1991. They had three children: Jennifer Ruth, John Frederick Jr., and Juliet Margaret. Johnny married Sharon Litsky in 1999.

Janet Sampson Reider

Janet was born March 1, 1937, in San Francisco. She's a photographer. A fine one. You can see her work at janetreider.com.

She met Pete Reider at Lowell High School when she was just thirteen. They married in the spring of 1958. They had three children: Jacob, Suzie, and Matt.

The Reider Family — Janet's Husband

Arthur Elliott "Pete" Reider

Pete was born November 1, 1936, in Topeka, Kansas. He went to Presidio Hill School, Grant School, Lowell High School, and then Harvard — Class of 1958. Then Harvard Medical School. He became a psychiatrist.

But before medicine, he was a runner. Captain of Harvard Track. He ran a 4:11 mile in 1957 — a Harvard record. His coach, Bill McCurdy, called him "one of the toughest little men I have ever known." He was inducted into the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982. Made the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team.

He practiced in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts, then retired to San Francisco. He published a book in 2014 called Stepping Stones — poetry and fiction. He was a member of the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation Lifelong Learning Institute at Brandeis.

He died at home on August 13, 2015, of lymphoma. Janet and their three children were with him. His memorial was at Congregation Emanu-El on August 17.

Dr. Norman Reider

Pete's father, Norman Reider, was quite something himself. Born in Alliance, Ohio, in 1907 — the same year I was born. He got his BA and MD from Western Reserve University, now Case Western Reserve. He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939. That's where Pete was born.

Norman headed the Department of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco. Among the founding members of the California Psychoanalytic Society. TIME magazine featured him in 1947 for his paper "The Unmarried Father." He wrote another paper called "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. His papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis.

He married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. She was a psychiatric social worker. Norman died in 1989. Louise died in 1985.

Norman's father was Maurice Jacob Reider, originally Rudner. Maurice came from Horoduk Village in the Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania. He worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio. His father was Uriah "Uri" Rudner, who was born in that same village in 1855. The family changed the name from Rudner to Reider.

The Places That Shaped Us

Oakland, California

My birthplace. Where the Kahn family built their store. Where my father served on the school board and helped found Temple Sinai. Where my mother established the foundation that bears our name. The Rotunda Building still stands at 16th and Broadway. A piece of our history, preserved.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Where the Sampsons came from. A small town with a surprising Jewish community. Where Joseph and Samuel Sampson were merchants and shipping operators. Where Joseph is buried in a cemetery established in 1772. The old South, before everything changed.

Galveston, Texas

Where Dr. AF practiced medicine. Where John was born. Where the family lived until the terrible flood of 1900 washed so much away. They rebuilt in San Francisco instead.

San Francisco

The city we made our home. Where the earthquake of 1906 dynamited AF's house. Where John and I raised our children in Seacliff. Where he practiced at Mount Zion and I volunteered there. Where I wove and attended concerts and kept going until the very end. Where most of the family still is.

Curaçao

A small island in the Caribbean where Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried. Where Matt went looking for answers about our Sephardic roots. Where the Beth Haim cemetery holds so many stories. Whether her son Isaac Marks is truly our ancestor, I cannot say with certainty. But her grave is there, carefully tended, section IV, row C, plot 621.

This history was composed in my voice by Matt, who wanted the family to remember where we came from.

— Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, "RoRo"

1907–1997
