The Sampson-Kahn Family

The Sampson-Kahn family traces its roots to 19th-century Jewish communities in Germany, South Carolina, and California. The family includes merchants, physicians, educators, and civic leaders who helped build Jewish institutions in Oakland and San Francisco.

The Kahn Family: From Darmstadt to Oakland

Henry (Hertz) Kahn and his wife Bela Goldstein lived in Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany in the early 1800s. Their son Israel Kahn was born there in 1822.

Israel Kahn emigrated from Germany in 1848, arriving in Baltimore aboard the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. He married Sarah Bauer (1828-1888). In 1879, Israel founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. He and Sarah had eight children: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, Frederick, John, Hannah, Bertha, Rosetta, and Henrietta. Israel died in 1883, Sarah in 1888.

Frederick Kahn was born September 21, 1860 in New York. He first worked at Bauer Brothers Company, a wholesale lace and fancy goods firm at 83 First Street in San Francisco, then joined the family store after its founding. In 1912, Kahn Brothers Department Store moved to a Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway designed by architect Charles W. Dickey. The building featured a 120-foot elliptical dome. It is now the Rotunda Building, Oakland Landmark #132, listed on the National Register.

Frederick served as first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay (founded 1918). He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai, chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education during a $5 million school building program, and vice president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. He belonged to the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith. Frederick died August 19, 1925 in Oakland and was buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

The Lavenson Family

Samuel Lavenson (1829-1900) married Caroline Heiman (1842-1872) in California. Caroline died June 24, 1872 in Sacramento at age 29 or 30, the same year her daughter Helen was born. She was buried at Home of Peace Cemetery, Sacramento. Samuel and Caroline had three daughters: Clara (born 1870), Sara (born 1871), and Helen (born 1872).

Helen Lavenson was born May 20, 1872 in California. On an unknown date, she married Frederick Kahn. After Frederick's death in 1925, Helen established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 to purchase 71 paintings of 19th-century California art for the Oakland Museum. The collection was catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian. Helen died March 4, 1935 in Oakland.

Albert S. Lavenson (1865-1930), Helen's half-brother or uncle (relationship through Samuel Lavenson), was born in Sacramento. In 1894, he married Amy Furth, daughter of Gold Rush merchant Simon Furth. Albert was partner and vice president of Capwell's Department Store for 30 years beginning in 1890. He served as president of Temple Sinai and organized the Commercial Club, which became the Oakland Chamber of Commerce. He supported music education at Mills College and was responsible for construction of the Music Building there. In 1927, he chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay. Albert died in 1930 at age 65 from heart disease. He left his estate to a charitable and educational foundation.

Albert and Amy had a daughter, Alma Ruth Lavenson (1897-1989), a noted photographer who studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham.

The Sampson Family: From England to South Carolina

The Sampson family came to America from Bury St. Edmunds, England. Joseph H. Sampson was born June 10, 1823 in Charleston, South Carolina. He became a prominent Jewish merchant and shipping operator in Georgetown, South Carolina, working with his brother Samuel. The family belonged to Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim in Charleston. By 1800, Georgetown had approximately 80 Jews, about 10 percent of the white population, making it the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen on an unknown date. Esther was born June 30, 1832 in Charleston and died September 22, 1897 in Georgetown. Her parents were Hartwig Cohen (1784-1861) and Deborah Marks Cohen (1801-1888). Hartwig Cohen was born November 14, 1784 in Wartha, Frankenstein, Silesia, Prussia. He served as spiritual leader of Charleston's Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim. He died October 12, 1861 in Winnsboro, Fairfield County, South Carolina.

After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel Sampson revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping. Joseph died July 14, 1883 and was buried in the Jewish Cemetery in Georgetown, established in 1772.

Arthur Fischel Sampson

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson was born July 1, 1855 in Georgetown, South Carolina to Joseph and Esther Sampson. He attended medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. On April 4, 1887, he married Babette "Bettie" Levy (born March 24, 1868 in San Antonio, Texas). Bettie's parents were Abraham Levy (1827-1879) and Esther Halff Levy (1833-1908).

After the 1900 Galveston flood, Arthur moved his family to San Francisco in 1902. The family home on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter was dynamited by the U.S. Army as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. They rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur and Bettie had five children: Joseph Abraham (born December 4, 1888 in Galveston), Arthur Jr. (born June 2, 1891 in Galveston), Cornelia "Nellie" (born August 8, 1893 in Galveston), Esther, and John Jacob (born April 28, 1898 in Galveston). Arthur died November 26, 1934 in San Francisco. Bettie died March 21, 1949 in Santa Clara, California.

Rose Etta Kahn and John Jacob Sampson

Rose Etta Kahn was born December 10, 1907 in Oakland, California to Frederick and Helen Kahn. As a girl, she carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication ceremony. She graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. She became an award-winning weaver, creating furniture upholstery for the family's Seacliff home and making a cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House.

Dr. John Jacob Sampson was born April 28, 1898 in Galveston, Texas. He earned his medical degree before age 21 after three years at UC, then served as medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India. He practiced medicine with his father, then joined the UCSF research and clinical staff. He became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital and practiced cardiology until retiring at age 85.

John published 49 papers between 1926 and 1980 on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman (who developed the Type A behavior theory), Herman Uhley, and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research. The John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology was established at UCSF in his honor.

Rose and John married May 17, 1931. After Frederick Kahn's death, Rose became president of the Kahn Foundation. She volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into her 80s. Rose died July 12, 1997 at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco from a lung embolism. A memorial service was held August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El. John died December 24, 1987 in San Francisco.

The Sephardic Ancestry Question

Deborah Marks Cohen's mother was born Deborah Marks. The family name was anglicized from Marques. Deborah was born May 3, 1801 in Charleston and died February 15, 1888 in New York. Her parents were Samuel Mendel Marks and Sarah Harris.

Samuel Mendel Marks was the son of Isaac Marks (1732-1776) and Miriam Simpson or Simons. Isaac Marks was born in 1732 in New York and died in 1776. He fought as a private in the New York Militia during the American Revolution and is listed in DAR family histories. He lived in New York City from age eight (1740). The name Marks was anglicized from Marques. Isaac was a cousin of Bernard Baruch through his granddaughter Sarah Wolfe.

Multiple genealogical trees list Isaac's mother as Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro, but this connection lacks primary documentation. Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro (possibly born December 16, 1699 in Bordeaux, France, or born 1710 elsewhere) died July 30, 1759 in Curaçao, Netherlands Antilles. She was buried at Beth Haim Cemetery, Curaçao, section IV, row C, grave 621.

Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722 in Barbados. Jacob died around 1725. She then married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro, who was buried in the adjacent plot (IV-C-620). Isaac Marks was born in 1732 with an unknown father. Esther also had a daughter, Rachel Marquez (born circa 1725 in Barbados).

The Levy Maduro family includes several documented rabbis. Esther's father was Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo (1673-1752), a rabbi born in Amsterdam who died in Curaçao. His father was Hazzan and Ribi Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro (1649-1708), a rabbi and cantor who married Hester Dias Sardo on July 13, 1670. Mosseh arrived in Curaçao in 1672.

Mosseh's father was Salomon (Salomão) de Moshe Levy Maduro (1629-1699), born in Amsterdam, who married Chana de Castro on October 27, 1648. Salomon's father was Mosseh Levy o Maduro (born circa 1591 in Portugal, died January 1, 1640 in Amsterdam), who adopted the Maduro name and married Clara (also known as Rachel or Raquel) Rodriguez on July 27, 1622 in Amsterdam.

Mosseh's father was Solomon Levy, whose father was Salomon (Samuel) Levy, whose father was Antonio Ha Levy Abulafia. Antonio's parents were Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz, who lived in Portugal in the 1400s.

However, there are contradictions in the available records. Some sources give Esther's birth year as 1699 and birthplace as Bordeaux. Other sources show a different Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro y Namias de Castro born in 1710 who married Isaac de Samuel Levy Maduro y Vieyra (1712-1784). The Geni genealogical profile may conflate two different women named Esther. The Bordeaux birthplace is unsupported, as the Levy Maduro family was based in Amsterdam and Curaçao.

The connection between Isaac Marks and Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro appears in multiple Geni and Ancestry trees but has not been confirmed by primary sources. Esther's first husband died around 1725, and Isaac was born in 1732 with no identified father. The parentage is plausible but unproven.

DNA testing conducted by Matt Reider showed 99 percent Ashkenazi ancestry, which raises questions about the extent of Sephardic heritage in this line. The Sephardic connection through the Levy Maduro family remains an open research question.
