The Sampson-Kahn Family

The Sampson-Kahn family traces its American roots to mid-19th century German Jewish immigration on the Kahn side and to early American Jewish settlement in Charleston and Georgetown, South Carolina on the Sampson side. The two families joined through the 1931 marriage of Rose Etta Kahn and Dr. John Jacob Sampson in San Francisco.

Kahn Origins: Germany to California

Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Kahn (née Goldstein) lived in Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany in the early 19th century. Their son Israel Kahn was born there in 1822. Israel emigrated to the United States in 1848, arriving in Baltimore aboard the Henry Shelton from Rotterdam. He married Sarah Bauer (1828-1888) and eventually settled in Oakland, California.

Israel Kahn founded a dry goods business in Oakland in 1879 at 10th and Broadway. He and Sarah had several children, including Frederick Kahn (born September 21, 1860 in New York), Samuel, Solomon, John, and daughters Hannah, Bertha, Rosetta, and Henrietta. Israel died in 1883, followed by Sarah in 1888.

Frederick Kahn first worked at Bauer Brothers Company, wholesalers of laces and fancy goods at 83 First Street in San Francisco. He later joined the family store. The business expanded significantly, and in 1912 Kahn Brothers Department Store moved into a new Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway designed by architect Charles W. Dickey. The building featured a 120-foot elliptical dome and is now known as the Rotunda Building, designated Oakland Landmark #132 and listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

Frederick served as first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay (founded 1918) and was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai. He chaired the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education during a $5 million school building program and served as vice president of the Alameda County Anti-Tuberculosis Society. He was active in the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith. Frederick died August 19, 1925 at his home at 3933 Harrison Street in Oakland. He was buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

The Lavenson Family

Samuel Lavenson (1829-1900) married Caroline Heiman (1842-1872). Caroline died on June 24, 1872 in Sacramento at age 29 or 30, the same year her daughter Helen was born. She was buried at Home of Peace Cemetery in Sacramento. Samuel and Caroline had three daughters: Clara (born 1870), Sara (born 1871), and Helen (born May 20, 1872).

Albert S. Lavenson (1865-1930), born in Sacramento, was Samuel's son from another relationship or marriage. Albert became a partner and vice president at Capwell's Department Store, where he worked for 30 years beginning in 1890. He married Amy Furth in 1894. Amy was the daughter of Gold Rush merchant Simon Furth. Albert served as president of Temple Sinai and organized the Commercial Club, which later became the Oakland Chamber of Commerce. He was a music patron at Mills College and funded construction of the Music Building there. In 1927 he chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay. Albert died in 1930 at age 65 of heart disease, leaving his estate to a charitable and educational foundation.

Albert and Amy's daughter Alma Ruth Lavenson (1897-1989) became a noted photographer. She was born in Oakland and studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham.

Helen Lavenson married Frederick Kahn. After Frederick's death in 1925, Helen established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 for 71 paintings, known as the Kahn Collection of 19th-century California art, to the Oakland Museum. The collection was catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian. Helen died March 4, 1935 in Oakland.

The Sampson Line: South Carolina to California

The Sampson family came from Bury St. Edmunds, England and settled in Charleston, South Carolina. Joseph H. Sampson was born June 10, 1823 in Charleston. He became a prominent merchant and shipping operator in Georgetown, South Carolina, working with his brother Samuel. Georgetown had approximately 80 Jewish residents by 1800, comprising about 10% of the white population and representing the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina. The town's Jewish cemetery was established in 1772.

Joseph married Esther "Hetty" Cohen (born June 30, 1832 in Charleston). Hetty was the daughter of Reverend Hartwig Cohen (1784-1861), spiritual leader of Charleston's Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim, and Deborah Marks Cohen (1801-1888). The Sampson name appears in the congregation's historical records. After the Civil War, Joseph and Samuel revived their mercantile business and expanded into shipping. Joseph died July 14, 1883 in Georgetown and was buried in the town's Jewish cemetery.

Joseph and Hetty's son Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson was born July 1, 1855 in Georgetown. He attended medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. On April 4, 1887 in Galveston, he married Babette "Bettie" Levy (born March 24, 1868 in San Antonio, daughter of Abraham Levy and Esther Halff). They had five children: Joseph Abraham (December 4, 1888), Arthur Jr. (June 2, 1891), Cornelia "Nellie" (August 8, 1893), Esther, and John Jacob (April 28, 1898), all born in Galveston.

After the 1900 Galveston flood, Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson moved his family to San Francisco in 1902. Their home on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter was dynamited by the U.S. Army as a firebreak during the 1906 earthquake. The family rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel. Arthur practiced medicine in San Francisco until his death on November 26, 1934.

Rose Etta Kahn and Dr. John Jacob Sampson

Rose Etta Kahn was born December 10, 1907 in Oakland. She graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. As a girl, she carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication ceremony. She became an award-winning weaver, creating furniture upholstery for the family's Seacliff home and making a cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. After her mother's death, Rose Etta served as president of the Kahn Foundation. She volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into her 80s.

John Jacob Sampson was born April 28, 1898 in Galveston, Texas. He earned his medical degree before age 21, completing three years at UC followed by medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter traveling to the Far East and India. John practiced medicine with his father before joining the UCSF research and clinical staff, where he worked until retirement at age 85. He became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital.

As a cardiologist, Dr. Sampson published 49 papers between 1926 and 1980 on topics including cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, and shock. He collaborated with Meyer Friedman (known for Type A behavior research), Herman Uhley, and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research. The John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology was established at UCSF in his honor.

Rose Etta Kahn and John Jacob Sampson were married May 17, 1931. Rose Etta died July 12, 1997 at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco of a lung embolism. A memorial service was held August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El. John died December 24, 1987 in San Francisco.

He attended Lowell High School, Reed College, and received his AB from the University of San Francisco in 1957 and attended Wharton in 1960. The Sephardic Ancestry Question

Multiple genealogical trees suggest a possible Sephardic Portuguese line through Deborah Marks Cohen, grandmother of Esther "Hetty" Cohen Sampson. The proposed connection runs through Isaac Marks (1732-1776), a private in the New York Militia during the American Revolution who appears in DAR family histories. Isaac lived in New York City from age 8 (1740). The family name was originally Marques, later anglicized to Marks.

Isaac's mother is listed in some genealogies as Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro (1699-1759), buried at Beth Haim Cemetery in Curaçao (section IV, row C, grave 621). However, Isaac's father is unknown. Esther first married Jacob Rodriguez Marques on May 27, 1722 in Barbados. Jacob died around 1725. She later married Ishac de Shemuel Levy Maduro (buried in the adjacent plot). Isaac Marks was born in 1732, seven years after Jacob's death, with no documented father.

The Levy Maduro family includes several documented rabbis and religious leaders in Amsterdam and Curaçao. Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo (1673-1752), Esther's father, served as a rabbi (Hazzan) and was born in Amsterdam. His father, Hazzan & Ribi Mosseh de Salomon Levy Maduro (1649-1708), also served as rabbi and cantor, arriving in Curaçao in 1672. The line continues back through documented ancestors to Mosseh Levy o Maduro (circa 1591-1640), who was born in Portugal and adopted the Maduro name after moving to Amsterdam, and ultimately to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in 15th-century Portugal.

However, significant gaps and contradictions exist in this genealogical chain. Emmanuel's books on the Jews of the Netherlands Antilles contain conflicting information about Esther's marriages. Some records show a different Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro (born 1710, not 1699) who married a different man. The connection between Isaac Marks and the Levy Maduro family, while appearing in multiple online family trees, lacks primary source documentation. Isaac's parentage remains unproven.

Matt Reider's DNA test showed 99% Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry with no detectable Sephardic markers. This ancestry question remains open for further research and verification.
