The Sampson-Kahn Family

Kept by RoRo - Rose Etta Kahn Sampson, 1907-1997

Our Family

I'm Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. Born in Oakland in 1907, I've spent my life knowing this family's story. I researched our tree with Jean Wildberg. This is what I know.

We come from merchants and rabbis, from Germany and South Carolina, from the Galveston flood and the San Francisco earthquake. We built department stores and hospitals. We carried forward what mattered.

The Kahn Family: From Darmstadt to Oakland

My grandfather Israel Kahn came from Darmstadt, Hesse, Germany in 1848. He sailed from Rotterdam on the Henry Shelton, landing in Baltimore. His parents were Henry (Hertz) Kahn and Bela Goldstein.

Israel married Sarah Bauer. In 1879 he founded a dry goods business in Oakland at 10th and Broadway. When he died in 1883, the business passed to his sons: Henry, Samuel, Solomon, and my father Frederick.

My Father, Frederick Kahn

My father was born in New York in 1860. He first worked at Bauer Brothers Company in San Francisco, then joined the family store. In 1912, Kahn Brothers Department Store moved to a grand Beaux-Arts building at 16th and Broadway. The architect Charles W. Dickey designed it with a 120-foot elliptical dome. That building still stands. It's now the Rotunda Building, an Oakland landmark on the National Register of Historic Places.

Father was the first president of the Jewish Welfare Federation of the East Bay. He was a founding member and president of Temple Sinai. He served as chairman of the Advisory Committee of the Board of Education, overseeing a five million dollar school building program. He belonged to the Concordia Club and B'nai B'rith. He died in 1925 at our home on Harrison Street. He's buried at Home of Eternity Cemetery in Mountain View Cemetery, Oakland.

My Mother, Helen Lavenson Kahn

My mother Helen was born in 1872, the same year her mother Caroline died. Caroline Heiman Lavenson was only twenty-nine when she passed, leaving three daughters: Clara, Sara, and Helen.

After my father died in 1925, Mother established the Kahn Foundation in 1928. In 1965, the foundation donated $125,000 to the Oakland Museum. That gift purchased seventy-one paintings of nineteenth-century California art, catalogued by Marjorie Arkelian. They call it the Kahn Collection.

The Lavensons

My mother's parents were Samuel Lavenson and Caroline Heiman. Caroline died on June 24, 1872, in Sacramento. She's buried at Home of Peace Cemetery there.

Mother's brother Albert was born in Sacramento in 1865. He was partner and vice president of Capwell's Department Store for thirty years. He served as president of Temple Sinai and organized the Commercial Club, which became the Oakland Chamber of Commerce. He was a music patron at Mills College and chaired the Jewish Welfare Campaign of the East Bay in 1927.

Albert married Amy Furth in 1894. Her father Simon Furth was a Gold Rush merchant. Their daughter Alma Ruth Lavenson became a noted photographer. She studied under Edward Weston and Imogen Cunningham.

The Sampson Family: Georgetown and Charleston

My husband John's family came from South Carolina. His grandfather Joseph H. Sampson was born in Charleston in 1823. Joseph and his brother Samuel were prominent Jewish merchants and shipping operators in Georgetown, South Carolina.

Georgetown had a significant Jewish community. By 1800, Jews made up about ten percent of the white population. It was the second largest Jewish community in South Carolina after Charleston. The Jewish cemetery there was established in 1772. Joseph is buried there. He died in 1883.

Joseph married Esther Cohen, called Hetty. She was born in Charleston in 1832. Her father was Hartwig Cohen, a spiritual leader at Kahal Kadosh Beth Elohim in Charleston. Hetty's siblings included Sarah Cohen Wolfe and Frances A. Cohen. Sarah was Bernard Baruch's grandmother.

The Sampson name came from England, from Bury St. Edmunds. It's an anglicization of the Hebrew Shimshon, meaning sun. The p was introduced in the Greek transliteration.

Dr. Arthur Fischel Sampson

Joseph and Hetty's son Arthur Fischel was born in Georgetown in 1855. He went to medical school in South Carolina and practiced in Galveston, Texas. There he married Babette Levy from San Antonio on April 4, 1887.

After the 1900 Galveston flood, Arthur moved his family to San Francisco in 1902. They lived on Franklin Street between Bush and Sutter. During the 1906 earthquake, the US Army dynamited their home as a firebreak. Arthur rebuilt on Washington Street near Laurel.

Arthur and Bettie had five children: Joseph Abraham, Arthur Jr., Cornelia (who we called Nellie), Esther, and John Jacob.

John and Me

I was born December 10, 1907, in Oakland. I graduated Phi Beta Kappa from UC Berkeley in 1928. As a girl, I carried the key at Temple Sinai's dedication ceremony.

John Jacob Sampson was born in Galveston on April 28, 1898. He got his medical degree before he was twenty-one. Three years at UC, then medical school. He served as a medical officer on a freighter to the Far East and India. He practiced with his father Arthur, then joined the research and clinical staff at UCSF. He didn't retire until he was eighty-five.

John became Chief of Staff at Mount Zion Hospital. He was a cardiologist. He published forty-nine papers between 1926 and 1980, research on cardiac pain, pulmonary lymph flow, angina, shock. He worked with Meyer Friedman, the man who defined Type A behavior. Also with Herman Uhley and Sanford Leeds at the Harold Brunn Institute for Cardiovascular Research.

We married May 17, 1931. I was an award-winning weaver. I wove the furniture upholstery for our Seacliff home. I made a needlepoint cushion for the Haas-Lilienthal House. I served as president of the Kahn Foundation. I volunteered at Mount Zion Hospital into my eighties, made weekly visits to nursery schools as Grandma RoRo, attended services and concerts at Congregation Emanu-El.

John died December 24, 1987. UCSF established the John J. Sampson-Lucie Stern Endowed Chair in Cardiology in his honor.

I died July 12, 1997, of a lung embolism at Mount Zion Hospital. My memorial service was August 4 at Congregation Emanu-El.

Our Children

John and I had three children.

Deborah

Deborah was born January 11, 1934. She's an educator and English literature professor. She went to Wellesley. She married John R. Green and they live in Seattle. Their son Caleb married Kate Banta-Green. Caleb and Kate have two sons, Robert Bruce and Peter Caleb.

John Frederick

John Frederick was born March 18, 1935. He went to Lowell High School, spent three years at Reed College, got his AB from the University of San Francisco in 1957, then went to Wharton. He's an appraiser and real estate broker in San Francisco. He married Louise Adler in 1962. They divorced in 1991. They had three children: Jennifer Ruth, John Frederick Jr., and Juliet Margaret. John married Sharon Litsky in 1999.

Janet

Janet was born March 1, 1937. She's a photographer. You can see her work at janetreider.com. She met Pete Reider at Lowell High School when she was thirteen. They married in the spring of 1958.

Pete Reider

Arthur Elliott Reider, called Pete, was born in Topeka, Kansas, on November 1, 1936. He went to Presidio Hill School, Grant School, and Lowell High School. Then Harvard, Class of 1958, then Harvard Medical School.

At Harvard he was captain of the track team. He ran a 4:11 mile in 1957, a Harvard record. He set two-mile records. His coach Bill McCurdy called him one of the toughest little men he'd ever known. Pete was inducted into the Harvard Athletic Hall of Fame in 1982. He made the All-Ivy League Cross Country Team.

Pete was a psychiatrist. He had a private practice in Cambridge, Hyannis, and Newton, Massachusetts, before retiring in San Francisco. He published a book in 2014, Stepping Stones, a collection of poetry and fiction. He was a member of the Fromm Institute at USF and the Osher Foundation Lifelong Learning Institute at Brandeis.

He died of lymphoma on August 13, 2015, at home with Janet and their three children. His memorial was at Congregation Emanu-El on August 17.

Pete's Father, Norman Reider

Pete's father Norman was born in Alliance, Ohio, in 1907, the same year I was born. He got his BA and MD from Western Reserve University, now Case Western Reserve. He trained at the Menninger Clinic in Topeka from 1934 to 1939.

Norman became Chief of Psychiatry at Mount Zion Hospital in San Francisco, succeeding Jascha Kasanin. He was an early member of the San Francisco Psychoanalytic Society. TIME magazine featured him in 1947 for his paper "The Unmarried Father." He wrote "Chess, Oedipus, and the Mater Dolorosa" in 1959. He served on the ACLU board in Northern California.

Norman married Louise J. Cohen in 1927. She was a psychiatric social worker. Norman's papers are archived at the San Francisco Center for Psychoanalysis, 4.6 linear feet covering 1822 to the 1980s. He died in 1989. Louise died in 1985.

Norman's father Maurice Jacob Reider was born Rudner in Horoduk Village, Vilna Gubernia, Lithuania. He changed the name to Reider. He worked as a foreman at a steel foundry in Canton, Ohio. His father was Uriah Rudner, born in 1855 in Orduk Village. Uriah's wife was Clara Scope.

The Sephardic Line: An Open Question

There's a possible Sephardic connection through Grandpa John's grandmother Hetty Cohen. Her mother was Deborah Marks Cohen, born in Charleston in 1801. The Marks name was anglicized from Marques, which suggests Portuguese Sephardic origins. But the links aren't proven.

Deborah's father was Samuel Mendel Marks. His father was Isaac Marks, born in New York in 1732. Isaac fought in the American Revolution as a private in the New York Militia. He married Miriam Simpson of Long Island. His son Samuel Mendel moved to Charleston.

Multiple genealogy trees list Isaac's mother as Esther de Salomon Mosseh Marques, also called Esther Levy Maduro y Namias de Crasto. She was born December 16, 1699, reportedly in Bordeaux, France. She died July 30, 1759, in Curaçao. She's buried at Beth Haim Cemetery there, section IV, row C, grave 621.

But here's the problem. Esther's first husband Jacob Rodriguez Marques died around 1725. Isaac was born in 1732. That's seven years later. Isaac's father is unknown. The documentation for this maternal link is unclear.

Bernard Baruch, the Wall Street financier, was a cousin through Isaac Marks' granddaughter Sarah Wolfe. He claimed descent from Isaac Rodriguez Marques in his autobiography. But the dates don't work for a direct line.

Matt researched this extensively in 2022. He corresponded with Ron Gomes Casseres at the Beth Haim cemetery in Curaçao and studied genealogist Isaac S. Emmanuel's records. Emmanuel's own books contain contradictions about Esther's marriages.

If the Sephardic connection holds, the Levy Maduro line goes back through rabbis in Amsterdam to Portugal in the 1400s. Esther's father was Ribi Hazzan Salomon de Mosseh Levy Maduro y Dias Sardo, a rabbi in Amsterdam. The line traces through several generations of Levy Maduros to Mosseh Levy o Maduro in Portugal, and ultimately to Diogo Rodrigues and Clara Roiz in the 1400s.

It's a fascinating possibility. But it remains a theory, not proven fact.

The Places That Shaped Us

Oakland

Oakland was home. Israel Kahn founded his dry goods business there in 1879. The Kahn Brothers Department Store building at 16th and Broadway still stands, a landmark with its great dome. Temple Sinai, where my father served as president, where I carried the key at the dedication. The Oakland Museum, home to the Kahn Collection. Home of Eternity Cemetery, where my father rests.

Georgetown, South Carolina

Joseph H. Sampson and his brother Samuel built their merchant and shipping business there after the Civil War. The Jewish cemetery, established in 1772, holds Joseph's grave. Georgetown had a vibrant Jewish community, ten percent of the white population by 1800.

Galveston

Arthur Fischel Sampson practiced medicine there. He married Bettie Levy there in 1887. Their children were born there: Joseph Abraham, Arthur Jr., Nellie, Esther, and John. The great flood of 1900 changed everything. In 1902, Arthur moved his family to San Francisco.

San Francisco

Arthur rebuilt after the 1906 earthquake took his Franklin Street home. John and I made our life there, in Seacliff. Mount Zion Hospital, where John was Chief of Staff, where I volunteered into my eighties, where I died in 1997. Congregation Emanu-El, where we held memorials for both John and me. UCSF, where John spent his career.

Curaçao

If the Sephardic connection is real, this is where it runs deepest. Esther de Salomon Levy Maduro is buried at Beth Haim Cemetery. Her father Ribi Hazzan Salomon arrived there in 1672. The cemetery, the synagogue, the records, they tell the story of Sephardic Jews who fled Portugal and Spain, who built new lives in the Caribbean, who kept their faith across oceans and centuries.

What Endures

Names change. Marques becomes Marks. Rudner becomes Reider. Shimshon becomes Sampson. People cross oceans, flee disasters, rebuild after earthquakes and floods.

What endures is what we carry forward. The businesses we build. The hospitals we staff. The schools we fund. The art we collect. The knowledge we preserve. The family we raise.

This is our family's history. I've kept it as well as I can.

How This Was Built

This site is generated by a WhatsApp bot named RoRo, after my grandmother Rose Etta Kahn Sampson. The idea started after I read a blog post about building a family tree AI assistant that connected a GEDCOM file to a WhatsApp bot using a graph database. I thought: I could do something like that for our family.

I already had a WhatsApp bot running on OpenClaw, an open-source framework for building AI personas on messaging platforms. My brother and I had been using it to chat with "Pepper," a bot modeled after our childhood dog. Pepper was just there for fun, nothing serious. But after reading that blog post, I wanted to build something more useful, something the whole family could interact with around a topic that actually matters to us: our history.

The first version of RoRo had personality but not much substance. I had some family facts saved in a couple of markdown files, and she ran as an OpenClaw agent. I asked her a dozen times, "Who are your grandparents?" and she had no idea. The information was too loosely structured for her to work with.

So I rebuilt her from the ground up. Instead of OpenClaw's higher-level framework, I went directly to the libraries it wraps: Baileys for WhatsApp connectivity and the Anthropic SDK for Claude as the language model. I moved the family data into a structured JSON file with proper fields for names, dates, places, relationships, and sources. That made a huge difference. RoRo could suddenly answer questions accurately and tell coherent stories about the family.

Next, I connected her to Geni.com through a small app I built for their API. This lets her look things up, validate what she knows, and push updates back when family members share new information. She can also export the tree as a GEDCOM file for use in other genealogy software.

Today, RoRo listens to our WhatsApp family group, answers questions about our history, and can update the family tree interactively. Changes get saved to the JSON, synced to Geni, exported to GEDCOM, and published to this site. She generates the narrative on the "Our Story" tab using Claude, and the whole site is deployed automatically through GitHub Pages.

I am about to introduce her new self to the family, with all these improvements. Wish us luck.
